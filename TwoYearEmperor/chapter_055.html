I sighed. "Fine, let's back up and make sure we've caught everything. Anyone notice anything else from what he's been talking about?"

"Here you are, Commander," Suze said, reaching forward to hand him a sheet of paper. We all leaned in to see what was on it; eyebrows went up as we found ourselves looking at a word-for-word copy of everything that had been said since Loki appeared, all written in a neat, feminine hand.

All eyes turned to Suze; she ducked her head a little but did not shrink into her seat this time.

I grinned and turned to fully face her. "The memory maiden to the rescue! I hereby promote thee to the rank of Imperial Executive Assistant, Order of the Golden Pen! Arise, Dame Suze!" With extended index finger, I tapped her ceremoniously on each shoulder. She blushed, but looked pleased.

Thomas watched my antics with a small smile, then turned back to reviewing the transcript. "Here it is," he said, jabbing his thick finger partway down. "Afi can only scry on his thing when he's within a mile of it. Loki said that Afi was able to use Scry Location on us because the Ice Assassin saw this place. That means that Afi himself must have been nearby."

Well, that didn't put a damper on the mood or anything.

"If he was here...why didn't he just kill us all?" asked Unn in confusion. "Instead of coming here himself, he could have used a Teleportation Circle to send in one of his Legions and overrun us. Especially if he's found a way to bypass our warders...."

"How did that work, anyway?" I asked curiously. I flipped through the Brainopedia, looking for the spells that Loki had mentioned. When I found the answer I started laughing.

"Oh, that's just wrong," I snickered. "Mind Blank prevents divinations from working on the subject. True Seeing is a divination, so Mind Blank keeps it from seeing the user if he's under Invisibility."

Unn was grumbling under her breath. "Most wizards don't know that trick; the Academy has worked hard to keep it quiet. We figured that Afi might know it, so we put up Invisible nets to prevent him just walking in."

My smile was a little bit twisted; I had to admire Afi's brilliance, although I didn't like the fact that it was aimed at killing me. "So he probably came in with a True Seeing of his own so that he could see the net, then teleported past it, or Shapechanged into an ant and walked under it, or something like that. Anyway, he did it. His Ice Assassin got in here, Afi looked around, and bam! we're open."

"It won't matter too much," Albrecht put in. "What he saw is the existing layout of the corridors and this particular instance of this particular Magnificent Mansion. It'll run out in a few hours and we'll cast another one. Even if it looks exactly the same, it will still be an entirely different plane and he'll need to get another look at it before he can scry us again. As to the corridors, we'll shift those around; we can keep the same layout but, as long as the Holes are connected in different sequence, it'll be outside his ability to scry without getting another look."

He gave a harumph of annoyance. "As to him getting another look...we'll just have to step up security a bit. Excuse me." He scribbled some orders on a piece of paper and ducked out into the hall to hand it to someone. Moments later, he was back.

"Thank you, Loki," I said, completely sincerely. "I know it wasn't easy for you to be so straightforward, but we really are grateful."

He gave a pleased grumble and went back to watching his portable TV. He was polite enough to use earbuds, at least.

"Ok, what else?" I asked, trying to move the focus back to what I viewed as the important point. "Anyone? Suze, Alpha Squad, if you have anything, chime in."

We all turned our eyes to the until-now-silent occupants of the room.

For a moment they all looked wildly uncomfortable, but eventually Aerith spoke up. "The colors seemed like the important part to me, M'Lords and Ladies. Why were His Majesty's colors like that, and what about the black flames and the normal-colored flames?"

"Hm," I hm'd. My brain wasn't spitting out an answer as fast as I would have liked, so I dispatched a slug of Scotch to lubricate the gears. "Mine wass probibly weird because I'm a real person, and outside your alignment system," I said thoughtfully.

"My assumption," Drifa began, speaking thoughtfully. "Was that the black flames were the basic color of the ritual...that they were the outward expression of the matrix that absorbs the sacrifices and converts them into the shell we're building for the Lady. The red flames would have represented a minor flaw in the ritual, a brief disruption after absorbing a sacrifice large enough to disturb the matrix."

The word 'matrix' brought a memory bubbling up from my carefully-locked-away subconscious. "The arcane matrix of a Polymorph Any Object spell is constructed using a Kafkan transformation field interleaved with a Delphic information-gathering matrix...," whispered the pompous voice of Isaac, immediately followed by a mushroom cloud blowing the top off Mount Herabu and vaporizing a man who had been loyal and proud to serve.

One panic attack and one Calm Emotions spell later, I was beginning to feel like something out of a Looney Tunes...something like the poor guy standing between a doctor with a Heal-O-Matic and a villain with a Mega-Bonkulator. "He's up! He's down! He's up! He's down! He's up! He's down!" I sent eighty or ninety thousand dollars worth of Scotch to shore up the defenses against such memories. I was fine now, of course, thanks to that never-to-be-sufficiently-praised Calm Emotions spell, but it seemed like a good idea to deploy some defenders in advance.

"By the way," I said, raising a finger in emphasis. "I've been forgetting suthing verr important. Things have been so nuts, what with the oceans of undead, an the eyeball stabbing an the gods an the whatnot. I've been forgetting, and tha's a verry bad thing."

I nodded decisively, pleased with how efficiently I had conveyed my point, and took a sip of Scotch in celebration. Looking around, I was suprised to find that everyone was looking at me expectantly.

"What have you been forgetting, Jake?" Thomas asked patiently. He reached for my bottle and I absently moved it out of his reach.

"Isaac and Reynard, an all the Languard who died on th way in here," I told him, confused at why I had to repeat this. "We need to get them rezrrected. Drifa, could you plis handle that?"

She looked uncomfortable. "I can, Your Majesty...but it might be better if I didn't."

I raised an eyebrow at her. "Whahuh?" I took a sip on my Scotch and was surprised to find the cup empty. Shrugging, I filled it up again and then took my sip. And another, just to keep it company. For some reason, Thomas got a pained expression when I did.

"Well," she began, looking more uncomfortable by the minute. "We can still do resurrection magic, of course, but it's started being...unwise. For the last year, every time we've resurrected someone, there's been...issues. Sometimes the dark gods have used the resurrection channel as a way to slip something into our world."

"Huh," I said, a bit owlishly. Resurrection sucks pretty hard. Suffocating...lungs burned to tatters. Eyes boiled, skin fried, eardrums burst, nasal passages scorched—total sensory deprivation. Brain was cooked like a pot roast...going to be stupid forever, just smart enough to know you're stupid....

Fortunately, we were at the tail end of the Calm Emotions spell when those memories word-associated through my brain. I quickly sent a warhammer of Scotch to break up the images so that I wouldn't have to deal with them when the time ran out in another twelve seconds. With valiant cries, my golden Scotch warriors charged to battle, burying my enemies in a pleasantly warm haze. It was nice.

"And, sometimes it's worse," Drifa continued, oblivious to my mental issues. "Sometimes the dark gods have done things to people's souls, in the afterlife. Sometimes the people come back...wrong."

I must have been pretty tired, because things seemed a little foggy and my head felt wobbly. I rubbed the sleep out of my eyes so I could focus on Drifa.

"Thash definitely na good, then," I declared firmly, making an effort to speak clearly through the sleep haze. (It was sleep haze. Really. Honest.) "We pro'lly shouldn't do that."

Thomas sighed and tipped his head in a signal to Rob. Rob's hand came down on my shoulder and he said "Neutralize Poison," in a voice that told reality that yes, this was the way it was going to be.

My lovely, horrible-memory-suppressing alcohol haze disappeared instantly, leaving all those aforementioned horrible memories of burning to death and being resurrected but not actually resurrected because the Landguard had to sacrifice their religion to a stuck up tinpot egomaniac god in order to pull me through time and I repaid them by conspiring with Albrecht to get them all killed which made Thomas torture me and ohgodohgodohgod....

"Calm Emotions," Bob said, doing a lovely job of not sounding exasperated or disapproving.

I sat up from my fetal hunch and calmly took another serving of the fried bananas that Loki had summoned with the meal. "Thanks, Bob. Anyway, where were we?"

"Are you sure you don't want me to fix that, Your Majesty?" Unn asked carefully.

I waved her off and kept enjoying the bananas. "Nope. No mucking with my head. I'll deal with the memories."

"Your Majesty—Jake, you are not dealing with the memories," Thomas told me gently. "That's the problem. It's getting worse, and the booze isn't helping; you really need to lay off that stuff. Why don't you give me the bottle?" He reached out his hand slowly, as though towards a skittish horse.

Without looking up, I moved the bottle to the side opposite him and tucked it in the crook of my arm. "I'm good, thanks. I can handle it; I'll just be more careful. And besides, if I do get hammered, Bob can always sober me up, right? Easy peasy, no problem."

"Jake, I'm with Thomas on this one," Albrecht said quietly. "You really need to do something about these panic attacks. I know you're nervous about mind magic, but perhaps we could—"

I looked up, eyebrow raised. I couldn't be angry at the moment, but there was no way I was letting Albrecht get away with that. "What is this, an intervention? Forget it, you people are not screwing with my head again. It's not happening, so stop suggesting it. Now, we were talking about resurrections and why they're a bad idea. Is there anything we can do about it?"

Uncomfortable silence reigned for several rounds as everyone resisted the topic change. I stared at them, calmly (of course) waiting for an answer.

"I...don't think so," Drifa finally answered. "The gods won't actually deny mortals access to spells or stop us from using them; if Neklos were to outright deny me the ability to use True Resurrection, the Overgods would crush him like a bug. And usually they don't...interefere with things. Right now though, with all the chaos in the world, it seems like some of the rules are being bent a little."

Loki snorted, not looking up from the bucket of fried chicken he was chomping on. (No, really—he was eating the bucket, not the chicken.) "'Being bent' if generof," he told us, cheeks packed full of cardboard. He chewed and gulped down the mouthful; I watched it distend its way through his throat and eventually disappear into his belly. There was something very disturbing about it.

We all waited patiently for him to continue, but he just went back to eating his bucket, tossing the chicken over his head so he could get at the cardboard better. (The various bits of chicken repeatedly reassembled themselves into clucking reddish-brown hens, which promptly disappeared.)

"Um...yes," Drifa said, nonplussed at the sight of Loki's antics. Clearly, she had not spent enough time around him if this was registering on her Weird-o-meter. "In any case...there's nothing that we can do to change what Neklos is doing with the resurrections. We can bring people back if it's important enough, but it's usually a good idea to use Programmed Amnesia on them afterwards, to make sure that their minds are put back the way they should be. And, sometimes the, soul-riders can be an issue."

I nodded, carefully focusing on this as an abstract problem instead of thinking about that whole 'death' and 'resurrection' and 'brain was cooked' thing which I absolutely was not thinking about at the moment, nope, definitely not.

"What's a 'soul-rider'? Something that hitchhikes back on the person's soul? What sort of thing are we talking about?" I asked carefully.

Albrecht shrugged. "We've had different ones. Sometimes it will be a demon that rides along with the resurrection, using it like a reverse Gate. Sometimes there will be a taint on the person's soul that works like a communicable form of mummy rot. Sometimes the dark gods have modified the mind attached to the soul, so the one resurrected returns as an agent of evil. Sometimes...well, the list goes on. The dark gods are very creative, and Neklos in particular is both cruel and evil."

"And terrible at chess," Loki added, not looking up from the game of Pokémon Emerald he was playing on his Trash80. "I mean seriously, the last time we played I gave him a rook and my king-queen pawns and he still lost! A rook and the king-queen pawns! Honestly, how do you lose with an advantage like that?"

He paused to sip his Dr. Pepper, and then added, "He's also a sore loser. Really terrible. Throws a hissy fit, even if you're nice about it." He put Pokémon on pause and glanced at us with a wicked smile. "Although maybe I shouldn't have done the victory dance. Or chanted 'you suck, I rule' at him. And the butt wiggling may have been a bit over the top."

He paused, looking thoughtfully at the ceiling and tapping his chin. "Yep. Maybe over the top. Still, blowing up an entire island? Probably a bit of an overreaction."

Without another word, he went back to having a holographic Bulbasaur pummel Squirtle on a machine that had been the cutting edge when I was in kindegarten. The rest of us just watched him, then turned back to our conversation, refusing to encourage him by giving him attention.

"I would really like to see Isaac and Reynard brought back," I told Drifa, feeling a little embarrassed as I said it. "I kinda owe them, for tricking them into killing themselves, and I'd like to make it right." All of the Deorsi except Albrecht were suddenly looking alarmed, so I hastened to reassure them. "Don't worry, I was psychotic at the time, but now I'm not."

The faces around me showed a remarkable lack of reassurance.

I sighed. "Look, this is why I'm so down on mind-editing spells. When I got here, Isaac, Reynard, and Matthew decided that I needed to be a better warlord, so they edited my brain. Took out fear, embarrassment...anything that might get in the way of me ruthlessly killing all of Flobovia's enemies. They cranked up the 'ruthless' part a little too high, though; when I figured out what they had done, I started planning how to kill them off and, um, how to kill off the Landguard for allowing it." I ducked my head a little as I glanced over at Thomas; he didn't react.

"Anyway," I hastened to add. "I tricked Isaac and Reynard into blowing themselves up, I banished Matthew to the furthest outpost in Flobovia—speaking of, Thomas, send a Sending to tell him he can come home. After all that, I talked with Albrecht about getting the Landguard killed." All of the Deorsi, again except for Albrecht, looked at Thomas with wide eyes, then looked back at me. "We never actually did anything about it," I hastened to add.

Albrecht raised an eyebrow. "Of course I did. You don't think we teleported into the middle of all those drauga by accident, do you?"

Thomas turned an icy gaze on him; a raised eyebrow invited further detail.

Albrecht just ignored Thomas's death glare and met his gaze calmly. "I made a formal treaty between nations. Part of my duties under that treaty were to get the Landguard killed. It would have been easy enough to load all of us onto flying carpets and use Teleportation Circle to appear a few miles above the city, then fly straight down. Instead, I sent all of you in on foot and waited until you'd taken enough casualties before my men and I pitched in."

The faint jingling of chain mail echoed through the room as Alpha Squad shifted their weight. The expression on Thomas's face was drifting back into that sheer blankness that he'd had when he stabbed Albrecht in the eye.

Albrecht shrugged casually, taking a spoonful more of the coconut pudding. "I'm telling you this now so that it doesn't come out at a less convenient time later." He finished his pudding, licked the spoon clean, and turned back to Thomas.

"Understand this," he told the Landguard leader. "I keep my deals, period. At that time, getting you all killed was part of the deal I'd made. Now, keeping you alive is part of my new deal. Do you have a problem with that, Commander?"

The silence creaked by like the wheels on a death cart. I could almost hear someone in the background chanting 'Bring out your dead! Bring out your dead!'...and not in the funny Monty Python way, either. Much more the actual "hey, two thirds of the population of Europe just dropped dead with seizures and gangrene. Throw the bodies on the cart so we can burn 'em before anyone else gets sick" sort of way.

"No. No problem," Thomas finally ground out. He considered Albrecht for another moment, looking as though he was measuring him for a coffin. "What about the resurrection issue? When you blackmailed us into annexing Anundjå, you told us that you would make your resurrection ability available to us."

Albrecht shrugged. "I have. What ability we have to resurrect people is at your disposal. I never said we had a lot of ability, or that it was a good idea to use it."

The table creaked where Thomas's fingers gripped the edge; I heard the faint pop! of wood starting to give way.

Albrecht's expression was suddenly just as lethal as Thomas's; the geniality that he wore as a mask fell away to reveal the centuries-old general and statesman. Leaning in, he looked Thomas directly in the eye.

"I protect my people, Commander," he asserted. "Whatever that takes. Yes, I have tricked and manipulated you and your government since the moment we met. I have lied to you, deceived you, destroyed two of your cities and most of their inhabitants, and gotten most of your men butchered by undead horrors. And now, you are my people, and all of my skills are turned to saving your ruler and your Land from an apocalypse. If you have a problem with me, do something about it now. Otherwise, stop acting like a petulant child and get over it."

The table's edge shattered in Thomas's fingers; aside from that, he was still as a statue. His face promised instant death to anyone who spoke, moved, or even so much as hinted at thinking about breathing too loudly. Alpha Squad waited in the four corners of the room, hands on their swords, ready and more than ready to butcher Albrecht and anyone who got in the way at a single word from their Commander.

After several of the longest seconds of my life, Thomas let go a shuddering breath and relaxed in his seat, placing the broken-off wood on the table with exquisite gentleness.

"Can we safely resurrect Archmagi Isaac and Reynard?" he asked, turning to Drifa with a tone of polite inquiry. "They are both intelligent and powerful, and would be a significant asset. Also, they are familiar with the Programmed Amnesia spell and could help to cleanse the minds of other people we resurrect. If we alternated between resurrecting high priests and archmagi, could we get ahead of the issues you've described?"

Drifa raised an eyebrow at Thomas's suddenly-calm affect but, seasoned adventurer that she was, she rolled right by it. "We can resurrect them, yes. We'll need to be very careful; they'll need to be rendered unconscious instantly upon revival, and then we'll need to verify and possibly correct their minds. We'll need to do multiple scans to find soul corruption, diseases, enchantments, and such. We'll also want to have a separate group of casters, divine and arcane, ready to contain any creatures that ride back on their souls."

She paused to calculate. "It will take about forty person-hours per resurrection, and all of those people will need to be high level personnel. Even with all those precautions it still won't be entirely safe. Something could come back with them, then immediately Plane Shift directly to Afi, or it could read an Explosive Runes bomb the moment it appears."

Thomas gaze got a bit distant as he thought through the process. "Yes, that makes sense. We can help with a lot of that; I'll have a squad onhand when you do the resurrection. They'll have readied actions to activate mage defense the instant the subject appears; that will drop them, the subject, and any soul-riders into a single Portable Hole where no magic or supernatural abilities work. They can render the subject unconscious and kill most riders quickly. That will free up some of your casters."

Drifa thought about that and nodded. "Yes, that should work. It won't significantly reduce the time though; the casters who are there to contain soul-riders are only needed for a few minutes. The majority of time is spent in the investigation and cleansing phases."

Turning to me, she continued. "Your Majesty, I want to be very clear: even with all of these precautions, this is not a safe procedure. No matter how much it would help increase our combat power, I strongly suggest against beginning mass resurrections. We've tried this route, with all sorts of precautions; no matter how careful we are, about one time in four we miss something. And in every case where we miss something, the results are very bad."

"Understood," I told her. "Still, I would very much like to have Isaac and Reynard back; I owe them big time. If you think that you can do it reasonably safely, please do. But the final call is yours—if you don't feel safe enough, we won't do it." That was a painful thing to say, but I'd always made it a policy not to gainsay expert subordinates when they were talking about their chosen field.

"Cough, cough. Questions? Asking thereof? Getting bored here," Loki announced. He was floating upside down, what looked like a graphic novel magically suspended in front of his face. I couldn't get a good look, but it seemed to feature some sort of greenish blob monster fighting marsupials.

"Right! Sorry, give us just one more minute, please," I told him with a guilty start. We all went into a quick huddle, debating what to ask next.

"I've got a list of questions that I'd like answered about this war," Albrecht offered, producing a thick sheaf of parchment from an inner pocket. "We could ask him 'what is the maximal set of numbers such that every element of the set is the numerical index of a true statement from the following list?'"

I shook my head. "No go. I tried something similar the last time he and I played this game, and he treated all of the statements as separate questions. He gave me a discount because he was in a good mood and liked the trick, but we don't have enough questions to burn on something like that."

Albrecht looked disappointed but nodded acquiescence. He left the list of questions on the table, and I took a moment to skim through the first few pages quickly.

"I think digging out more about the ritual should be the top priority," Drifa suggested. "If it's not going to work, we need to relocate Grǫfhamr now, before Afi's troops show up. If it is, then we need to know more about what Herrún will be like when she reifies."

Thomas was frowning. "The military situation seems more immediately important to me. Afi has Legions standing by, ready to deploy. He knows where this place is; he could have dropped millions of undead on us already, but he hasn't. Why not?"

Albrecht shrugged. "They can't teleport closer than six miles; it will take them an hour or two to march that distance. We have a little time yet. We can push as many people through the ritual as possible, and get as much information from Lord Loki"—he paused to give the trickster god a polite nod—"and then decide if we need to move Grǫfhamr or not."

"Personally, I'm most interested in knowing more about the gods in general," I put in. "Why are they trying to wipe out the world? Why aren't the gods of life shutting down the undead? Also, it seems clear that there's a correlation between power of the god and number of worshippers, but which way does that flow? What exactly are the limits on them, and why? If this Neklos is driving the drauga's expansion, is he pitching in? If not, why not? The gods are the root cause of everything we're dealing with, so if you want strategic information"—I gave Albrecht a significant look—"then that's it."

"Seriously bored now, guys," Loki called, an edge coming into his voice.

"Sixty seconds, please!" I called.

The following sixty seconds was the fastest and most intense conversation I've ever had but, eventually, we were ready. The words weren't polished, but I was keeping all of Unn's earlier advice in mind and mentally reviewing what I was going to say.

"Ok, we're ready," I declared, breaking the huddle and turning to face Loki. He was currently being a zebra, but I didn't let that stop me.

I picked up the inkwell and took a deep breath, pondering my wording carefully. "I'd like to use one of the 'honest and complete' questions, please." I paused to review it again, licking my lips nervously; I really did not want to screw this up.

"What is the set of words in a language that everyone at this table understands that contains all the truths that we most need to know and that you will award us for the cost of only a single question, sorted by importance from most important to least important?" This had seemed just different enough from the 'which of these is true' that it was worth taking a chance on. And, worst case, it would only cost a single question.

Loki stared at me, frowning and tapping one hoof in irritation. I gulped and carefully stared at his chin.

Suddenly his face lit up in a happy equine grin. "Sure, no problem," he said, somehow forcing coherent speech out of a mouth that was never intended for it. "I'll even write it down for you for easy reference. Here you go." He reached into the pocket of his overalls and tossed me an old-fashioned brass key.

Startled, I fumbled the catch and had to pick the key up from the floor. I turned it over in my hands, confused.

"What's this?" I asked.

"Look behind you," he said with a grin. I glanced back; a wooden door hovered, unsupported, two feet behind me. It was painted white with sky blue trim, and it had a large brass doorknob and keyhole, clearly a match for the key in my hand.

Carefully, I got up and unlocked the door. Thomas rose to stand beside me but Loki gestured casually and he was slammed back into his chair.

"Don't worry, it's safe," Loki said casually. Thomas struggled for a moment and then sat, tensely watching me.

I opened the door and looked inside. Behind the door was a room lit by buzzing, flickering fluroescent lights. Three tall bookcases filled the room, leaving just enough room to slip between them. The shelves were jam packed with tomes, each a yard high and wide and at least six inches thick. The impressiveness of the books was completely overwhelmed, however, by the impressiveness of the room. It went on, and on, and on, receding farther and farther away until it hit the vanishing point. I was pretty sure it went on from there.

"These books contain everything you should know about everything you've expressed interest in," Loki said smugly, leaning back in his seat and crossing his arms. "It's all here...the enemy, the gods, whatever. Enjoy."

I knew perfectly well that there was a catch, but I pulled down the first volume from the first shelf and flipped it open. Aside from the margins, the pages were completely black.

I looked at Loki with an inquiring eyebrow, cocking the book so everyone could see. "What's this?" I asked again.

Loki's grin stretched so wide it met behind his ears. "Well, there's a lot of info! I had to use a small font...I'm sure you can find an electron microscope around here somewhere." He snickered a bit. "Oh, I'm afraid it's a bit wordy. That first volume you're holding there? That contains the opening paragraph of the discussion about the nature of the gods. The rest is in the books from the next nine linear feet.

"And don't think about using Scholar's Touch or some other speedreading magic," he told us, wagging a finger in disapproval. "That room is an antimagic zone, and the books are immune to magic. Still! It's everything you asked for, Jake! Honest, very complete, and non-deceptive! All of my juiciest info, laid out for your perusal. Enjoy!" He crossed his legs, his grin so utterly smug that it must have smugly cornered the market on smugness and completely shut down the ability of anyone else in the entire multiverse to look smug for the next century.

"Come on," he said, waving his hands in a Matrix-esque 'come and get it' gesture. "That was fun. Be with the asking of your next one." The scoreboard behind him dinged, leaving us with only six 'honest and complete' answers.

I stepped out of the room and walked around a bit, watching over my shoulder as the doorway kept a steady distance of two feet from me, expanding the room whenever it needed space.

"Don't forget to lock up," Loki reminded me. Shrugging, I put the key in the lock and turned it; the door instantly vanished. Suspecting how this worked, I stuck the key into thin air and turned it the other way; sure enough, the door reappeared and clicked open.

"Right, thanks," I said in disgruntlement. I locked up again, resumed my seat, and rolled my mug around in my hands, trying to think of the next question. At least he was in a good mood now.

I turned back to the others to figure out what to ask, only to be interrupted by Loki's throat clearing. "Like I said, bored now," he told me sternly. "If you want more questions answered, ask 'em yourself. I'm tired of waiting while all of you natter on."

I gulped and glanced at the others. Unn made a 'well, go on!' gesture at me, so I turned back to Loki and gathered my thoughts quickly. It didn't take long to figure out the right question.

"I'd like to use a 'truthful but maybe deceptive' answer, please," I told him. It was a risk, but the question was simple and I hoped that Loki wouldn't muck around too much. Also, it would give him a break from being so open, since that was obviously a problem for him. I was all in favor of anything that would put him in a better mood.

"If Afi was in here, he knows that we're doing the ritual," I began. "He was probably able to Detect Thoughts the details out of someone, so he probably knows what it's about, too. Could he do the same ritual himself and resurrect her in his own location, grateful to him?" It was important; I needed to know if we were in a race to finish our ritual before he finished his. And I figured that, if I had the slightest idea that Loki got tricky with his answer, we could always just discard what he said and burn an 'honest and complete' question.

"Hm," Loki said, standing up so that he could pace back and forth, hands clasped behind him in thought. "Well, simply put, no. Starting a ritual like this requires abilities that undead simply don't have. So, no, he can't do his own copy of this ritual on his own ground and resurrect her before you finish yours, which is what I figure you really wanted to know." The scoreboard behind him went ding! and decremented to '4'.

I digested that. It seemed pretty straightforward, but this was Loki. I would accept the answer as evidence, but assign a relatively high probability that there was something sketchy about it.

"I'd like to use another 'truthful but maybe deceptive' answer, please. Is the following statement true or false: the blackness of the ritual fires represents some facet of the ritual magic itself, as opposed to being caused by an externality."

Loki smiled. "False," he told me, in an uncharacteristic display of straightforwardness. The board behind him bing!ed like the horn of the apocalypse.

I went white as a sheet. "Oh, crap...."

Loki bowed from his seat. "Seems like you have some things to talk about," he snickered. "I'll leave you to it. Tata!" With a fanfare of vuvuzelas in F# Major, he vanished.

This was definitely a leemer moment, but I forced my way through it and turned to face the others. "Albrecht, I think this is the time to deploy that little surprise your apprentice magi have been taking care of. Like, right now."

Everyone else had gotten it too, because they were looking just as pale as I was. Albrecht nodded jerkily. "I'd say we should also try unloading the Portable Holes. First one, then the other."

It seemed like a good idea but, even if it hadn't, I wasn't about to spend time arguing with him. I nodded sharply and we all headed out the door one step short of a dead run.
