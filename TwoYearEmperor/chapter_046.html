I grimaced; he'd seen right through me. "Ok, fine. I won't buy more than a hundred a day, and I'll keep it proportional, I promise." I sighed. It had been such a beautiful plan.

He grinned and waved a hand. Reality flickered around me like a glitch in the Matrix; after the flicker he was in a slightly different position and his glass was empty. "There you go. I also packaged them up for you, and had one of my team come in to do the cleric spells. You're all set."

I looked back at the nearest wagon; the original contents were gone, replaced with a stack of what looked like telephone poles, all covered in heavy tarps.

He stood up, patting down the pockets of his robe like he was checking that he had his keys and wallet. Nodding in satisfaction, he waved and faded away.

I stared at where he'd been for a moment and stood up. As soon as we were all on our feet, the bar and stools vanished.

"Oh, one last thing—" Arros said, shimmering back into existence. "You can keep the bottle." He pressed the container of perfection into my hands and vanished again. I clutched it tightly to my chest, eyes shining in delight.

~= now =~

"What's causing those explosions?" Albrecht asked curiously.

"Robert," I responded. "The ammo is eighty rubber balls wrapped up in clay and baked in a Permanencied Wall of Fire spell. We put fifty Celestial Brilliance spells on each of the clay cylinders; since the balls are inside it, they are affected too. When the cylinder hits the ground it shatters and the rubber balls scatter everywhere. Each of the balls has Explosive Runes on it. Robert is Greater Invisibilied and Flying over the battlefield with Eyes of the Eagle and a telescope. Whenever a bunch of the balls come to rest he looks down, reads the Runes on one of them, and the explosion blows them back into motion." I shrugged. "It also tends to destroy a few of the balls, but the ones that survive continue doing damage."

I nodded to Colborn, emphasizing that I was making an important point. "I said we could each choose three people. You didn't bother choosing any; you're about to see what me and my 'meatshields' can do."

"Yes, yes, very amusing I'm sure," Colborn sneered. "Can we get on with this? You obviously just cheated, so you lose the bet."

My eyebrows shot up in feigned dismay. "Cheated? You slur my honor, sir! How have I cheated?" Out of the corner of my eye, I saw Albrecht fighting to restrain a smile. He knew exactly where this was going.

"You cast Launch Object on your toy over there," he said, gesturing to the cannon. "And you cast Celestial Brilliance on the ammunition. Obviously. The deal was that none of you would cast any spells, so you cheated and you lose." He turned to Albrecht with a satisfied look on his face, withdrawing a folded piece of paper from his robes. "Now, I've got the list of which magi and which meatshields I'll be taking with me. We'll load provisions tomorrow and leave first light the next day. I'll try to send reports back periodically and I'll tell you where to attack. Don't contact us, as the Sendings could potentially arrive at an inconvenient moment and blow our cover if we're sneaking."

I laughed. "I said that neither I nor my three-man team would cast any spells. We didn't—Arros and his teammate cast the Explosive Runes and Celestial Brilliance spells. Jary here"—the young mage shrank back as all eyes turned to him—"cast the Fly and Invisibility spells. The four of us didn't cast a darn thing."

He gave me a fulminiating glare which I thoroughly ignored. Instead of saying anything further, I just paced over to Thomas and climbed up on his back, piggyback style. Looking over my shoulder at Albrecht and Colborn I announced. "Albrecht, let us know when time is up, ok?" The pudgy little general nodded, his face practically radiating curiousity.

I turned to Colborn to put the capstone on things. "Remember I said there were some other differences between us? Well, one of the more important ones is that y'all are only allowed to say one command word every six seconds, but I can talk as fast as I want." I tapped my heels against Thomas's sides and shouted, "Go!"

Thomas took off, running like greased lightning past the rows of cannon. He carefully stayed hunched over so as to keep his (and my!) head below the barrel openings. Duncan came a few yards behind us, his phone poles stuck out to the sides so that they passed over the troughs on the barrels.

It was an intricate ballet, and we had had only a few hours to practice, but they pulled it off flawlessly. As we went I was bellowing "Fire Shrink Fire Shrink Fire Shrink Fire Shrink Fire Shrink Fire Shrink Fire Shrink Fire Shrink!" as fast as I could and at the top of my lungs. Every time I said 'Fire', all cannon within ten feet blasted their brightly shining ammo out over the valley below us. Every time I said 'Shrink', all of those firing rods contracted back to their ready state.

Of course, the great weakness of cannon is that it takes time to reload them. This is where I was beating on Stupidworld's rules like a speed bag at a boxing gym. The columns that Duncan was carrying were essentially giant rifle cartridges, each one containing eighty rounds of ammunition stacked vertically. He held the column over the cut-away barrels; every time the cannon reset he would drop the next ammo load out of the appropriate column and into the barrel's trough. The steady 'thwang!' of the firing rods clashing off the backplates of the cannon and the flashbang-bright light that surrounded each of those ammo cannisters made for an almost unbearable sensory overload, but Thomas and Duncan kept it together.

There was no way on Earth that this would have worked...or, rather, there was no way that this would have worked on Earth. But we weren't on Earth, and Stupidworld played by entirely different rules. Here, you could run forwards, backwards, or sideways at your full speed. Dropping things was a free action, so Duncan could drop a hundred cannisters of ammo over the course of a round as easily as one, and given Duncan's Dexterity score, each ammo cannister always fell right into the trough of a cannon, ready to be fired.

It was totally nuts, but it worked.

When the two of them got to the end of their run, Duncan dropped the expended ammo cartridges and pulled the next two into position, one in each hand. The two of them pivoted on a dime and started back through on another sweep. Picking the ammo up would have taken a move action, meaning that Duncan would have had to move normally instead of running that round, but he didn't have to pick them up—he was already carrying them and so just had to reposition them in his inventory. We were firing off eighty cannon every six seconds, each of which threw around eighty undead-vaporizing rubber balls on a slightly different trajectory out over the seething horde of undead below. The balls were awesome enough that I had decided they needed their own special title. I had debated calling them "Emperor's balls" but decided that was maybe a little inappropriate. On further thought, I decided that they should be named for the ruler of Venice instead of the ruler of Flobovia. Thus were born the Doge-balls. (Bum dum tish!)

"Time!" shouted Albrecht. Immediately, Thomas and Duncan dropped their cartridges and raced rapidly up to the crest of the hill where Albrecht and Colborn stood so that we could see the effects of the shoot.

I glanced at Colborn and then turned back to Albrecht with a tight-lipped smile. "So, do you think we killed more than sixty-two thousand?"

Albrecht stared down at the virtually depopulated valley below us in wonder. "Oh my, yes," he whispered in a voice filled with awe.
