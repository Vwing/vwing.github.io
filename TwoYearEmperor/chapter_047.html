Colborn turned to me, mouth hanging open in shock.

"..." he said.

I smiled.

"...but..." he tried again.

I decided to take pity on him. "I forgive you your debt, Colborn," I told him respectfully. "You are a Master High Mage of Anundjå; you should not have to embarrass yourself like that. I would very much appreciate your help with this campaign, if you're willing?" I paused and grinned, inviting him to join in the laughter. "Although, if you wanted to tell some people 'Jake was right!' I wouldn't mind."

His mouth clopped shut; he stared a moment, then gave a jerky nod. "Thank you," he managed to say.

I clapped him on the shoulder. "Now, I suspect you're tired of being human and would prefer to go back to your original form. It'll have to wait until we're back at Grǫfhamr, I'm afraid, Polymorph can't turn a human into an undead, Mage Jary here isn't high enough level for Polymorph Any Object, and Albrecht told me before we came that he didn't have it prepared. Anyway, let's get back and we'll get you fixed up."

Colborn shook his head. "No reason to waste a Polymorph Any Other when I can just do it myself," he said. I started to ask what he meant, but before I could get the words out he drew his dagger. Without a moment's hesitation, he rammed the needle-like point up through the soft underside of his jaw, jamming it into his brain and killing himself instantly. His body collapsed in a flacid heap on my feet.

"Urgh!" I yowled, jumping back in horror.

The body shimmered; the teeth elongated into fangs, the muscles filled out, the potbelly retreated into nothing. A moment later Colborn opened red-pupiled eyes, reached up, and yanked the dagger out of his head. The wound in his jaw sealed itself over in an instant, and he climbed back to his feet, brushing the dust off his robes.

"What?" he asked, looking confused at my gaping expression. "Everyone knows that if you're killed while Polymorphed, you revert to your original form. Still dead, of course, but then again...I'm already dead." He gave me a fang-baring grin and turned to Jary. "Shall we go?"

"Thiph if amaphing," said Master Sage Einarr, wolfing a mouthful of pastry. He took a gulp from his tankard—it was intended to hold ale, but he had filled it with sixteen ounces of a remarkably good red wine.

Einarr chomped down the last bite of his delicious flaky-crusted meatpie, glorying in the it's-not-created-protein-glop flavor of it. Even while he chewed, he was reaching for a fresh, juicy apple; the moment his mouth was clear he took a huge bite. The crisp snap! of it was audible around the table.

I smiled, topping off my own tankard from what I was starting to call the "Everful Bottle of Liquid Perfection, +27 Bazillion." I drew in a breath of its heady Scotch bite, then took a good long pull. It went down like an amazingly inappropriate simile and I could feel its warm glow reddening my face. I took another sip, savoring the perfection of it.

Colborn sat across the table from me, next to Albrecht. He eyed our food and booze and looked glum...and a little nauseous. "I remember enjoying food and alcohol," he said sadly. "Now all I can eat is blood and, honestly, the smell of all this stuff is making me queasy. I don't suppose you could put it away, could you?"

Einarr shook his head and reached for a hunk of chocolate cake with his left hand while shoving another bite of the apple in with his right. "Noph," he mumbled around the cake that was now half in and half on his mouth.

Albrecht smiled. "I'm afraid the Master Sage has spoken, grandnephew. You will have to, ah, 'cope and deal', as Jake says." He smiled in amusement as he watched Einarr pack away the calories. (Another bit of unfairness! Stupidworlders had no connection between calories and weight. They could eat bon-bons and fudge ripple ice cream all day and not gain a pound. Bastards.) "In fairness, this is the first decent meal he's had in six years." He took a sip from his wine goblet, savoring it for a moment. "Also, to be honest, I'm enjoying it myself. We ate well in Flobovia, but I was back here so much that I was eating glop four meals out of seven."

That surprised me. "Why were you back here so much? And, for that matter, how? When we all teleported in, the place was totally invested with undead." I winced a little as I said that, pushing the images aside. I took a pull on my tankard, focusing on the perfect rasp of the alcohol as a distraction from the thoughts of burning and dying friends, living corpses, pain, torture...I shook it away and took another slug.

He shrugged. "Until recently we were located elsewhere, up around the northwest corner of Nordfjall. That's where I was going to and from." He paused to take a delicate bite of the crispy, flaky shepherd's pie—lots of minced beef, carrots, celery, onions, garlic, ginger, hints of spices, all cooked in a red wine sauce with a heavy layer of mashed potatoes and cheese over the top. "A few days before you and I came here, a patrol of undead stumbled over us; pure bad luck as far as we can tell, but that doesn't matter. Anyway, we had to move. We hadn't found a new location yet, so we were forced to fall back on Snorri's cavern. Afi found it years ago; we just didn't expect him to still be keeping an eye on it. Shortly after Grǫfhamr moved, he must have heard that we were back and deployed one of his Legions on it." He took a nibble of the chocolate cake, then added, "We're actually quite lucky we came in when we did. Most likely, Afi would have attacked the next day and Grǫfhamr might well have been captured or destroyed. He wouldn't have deployed such a large contingent unless he had figured a way around the Celestial Brilliance fence."

The idea of the undead flooding into Grǫfhamr—possibly into this very room—spiked through my brain. My unfortunately perfect recall dredged up the weight of the Landguard head that had bounced off my back, the feel of the wight tearing my ribs open and sucking the life out of me, the smell of rotting corpses everywhere that were still attacking us, the sight of chunks of Franklin's face and arms being ripped off even as he fought...

A strangled cry ripped its way out of me as I shoved myself back in my chair. My chest heaved in fast, shallow gasps, my heart was pounding so hard I thought it would smash through my ribs; cold, clammy sweat burst out over my entire body, making me feel like I'd fallen under the ice on a river. The world went sparkly and white around me as I folded into my chair, pulling my knees up under my chin, hiding my face and whimpering like a lost child.

"Calm Emotions," Duncan declared flatly.

I sat up, took a bite of one of the pastries, and had a slug of Scotch. "Thanks Duncan, that's much better," I commented. Turning back to my Strategos, I followed up. "Albrecht, I meant to ask—you were conquering all our towns and villages and leaving detachments behind to run them. How did that work if you only had a few thousand soldiers?"

Everyone was staring at me as though I'd grown an extra head.

"What was that?" Thomas asked, confused. "You looked like you'd just been hit by a Fear spell."

I shrugged, all concern walled away behind the magic glass wall of Duncan's spell. "Panic attack, I guess. Anyway, I'm fine now. Although, it would probably be good to make sure there's always a few Landguard on hand with that Calm Emotions spell. Albrecht, you were saying?"

"You realize this only lasts a few rounds, right?" Duncan grated. "And only as long as I'm concentrating?"

"Oh," I responded. "Hm. Well, that may prove to be awkward. I suppose we'll have to find a better solution then. Don't know what that might be, but I'll think about it." I paused, spinning a few ideas around, but nothing seemed to click. After a bit I gave up. "Ah well," I shrugged. "I'll just have to deal with it as it comes. If it gets too bad you can always use Calm Emotions to help me through the worst of it. Anyway, where were we?" I took another sip of my Scotch, enjoying the rich and heady scent.

Silence reigned for a long moment. "What are 'panic attacks'?" Thomas inquired cautiously.

I sighed, shaking my head. "Of course you don't have those here. For you people, fear is just a bloody status effect. Grr." I pondered the unfairness of it all, then explained. "Panic attacks are a response that real people have to traumatic events. Symptoms are heart palpitations, tremors, difficulty breathing...basically, panic. Anyway, they'll probably keep happening." I thought back, dredging up the few facts about the condition that I'd read online, mostly out of curiousity after it was mentioned on a favorite TV show. "Let's see...best treatment is cognitive behavior therapy, which I doubt anyone in this world knows squat about. I don't suppose you have any benzodiazepines, do you? No? Yeah, didn't think so. Hm. I think aversion therapy had some success, maybe we could try that? Hm."

After a few more moments of unsuccessful thought, I shrugged. "Let's get back to work," I continued. "Albrecht, you were saying?" I poured down a long gulp of Scotch to help burn up the few traces of concern that were leaking around the Calm Emotions and distracting me. It worked so well that I sent another after it as reinforcements.

Albrecht studied me for a moment before continuing, but clearly decided to let it lie for now. "We conquered the population centers mostly so that we could have free run of their libraries and reference materials, as part of the search for Herrún's temple," he offered. "And, secondarily, to lend credence to the idea that we were the powerful invading force that I was framing us as." He paused for a bite of cake before continuing. "After we reduced Oxport and Tor Canle"—Thomas's mouth tightened—"the rest were eager to surrender, so we didn't have to fight for them. We immediately shipped their armed forces back to Anundjå and left a small detachment of our own people behind to rule. Usually just half a dozen or so, to serve as administrators, but we created a permanent Teleportation Circle leading to each town. I had two companies dedicated to constantly shifting through the various Circles and marching around, making it look as though we'd left significant forces in each place. We were careful to treat the inhabitants well—plenty of medical care, education, fair legal enforcement—and integration started pretty quickly." He shrugged apologetically. "It's pretty standard practice in such situations; we've been dealing with province management for hundreds of years. There are standard drills for this kind of thing."

I frowned. "How could you create a permanent Teleportation Circle to each town? You were moving around constantly." My words seemed to come from a little ways away, shielded from me by a warm soft fog. It was nice.

He shrugged. "A Teleportation Circle can be inscribed on any surface...including a tarpaulin. All you have to do is put it on the tarp and then you can fold it up and carry it with you. That's how we usually do them, for exactly that reason."

We all stared at him as he casually rewrote warfare and then casually ate a bite of blueberry pie.

After a moment, I shook it off. "Ah...ok. So. We wiped out a Legion of Afi's troops," I nodded to Colborn and he gave me a smile and a nod in return; the smile was a bit tight-lipped, but at least he appreciated the humor. "We've got a store set up here so that everyone can get decent food. I brought a lot of the Flobovian archives and spell library back, along with the stuff we copied from Herrún's temple." I grinned and nodded to Suze where she sat slightly behind me. "And, of course, my inimitable and lovely assistant, who categorically refused to be left behind. Anyone ever tell you you're scary when you're determined, Suze?" I asked, teasing.

She blushed and looked at where her hands were twisting in her lap, but I could see a tiny little smile of pride. "You told me before that you needed my help, M'Lord." She paused, and then continued in a very small voice. "And...I've never been outside the city before. I wanted to see what it was like."

The Landguard chuckled—I'm sure that part of it was pride in how Suze, who had become something of a mascot for them, was finally coming out of her shell. I'm also sure that another part of it was at the memory of Suze refusing to be left behind, up to the point of standing in the doorway with her arms folded and a mulish look on her face, arguing with me about why she needed to come and wagging her finger at me in determination. At the time, I could tell that she was nervous and would probably have crumbled if I'd really pushed it, but I just couldn't say no to that face. And besides, it was good to see her finally developing some self-confidence; I didn't want to crush her first ever attempt at being assertive...no matter how much she looked like a growling puppy.

"And see things you shall, my oh-so-fierce memory maiden," I promised, lofting my mug high in a toast. Everyone joined me and we all gulped a bit of our various drinks in congratulations. Suze blushed tomato red and looked at her lap, shoulders hunched in pleased embarrassment.

I relented from teasing her any further and went back to my prior thought. "Let's see...Legion destroyed, store established, documents and spell library brought back...." I smiled secretively. "And, of course, my little surprise is ready and waiting. You assigned the apprentice magi to take care of it, right?" I waited for Albrecht's nod before continuing. "Ok, I think that takes care of all the old business. What's next on the hit parade?" I took another long pull on my Scotch; god that was good. I needed to send a personal thank you to Arros for letting me keep the bottle. I sighed, awash in delight at my full belly, the hint of warm Scotch fog, and the company of smart people. Even in the middle of a war, life could be good.

Albrecht was clearly becoming accustomed to my incomprehensible idioms, because he shrugged it off and stayed on track. "Next is resurrecting Herrún; Master Sage, perhaps you could fill us in?"

Einarr froze like a deer in headlights. He had a drumstick in one hand, a piece of cake in the other, and a slightly guilty look on his face as he finally recognized his ill-mannered gluttony.

"Mmph," he mumbled, setting down the chicken and cake and washing his mouthful down with some wine. He wiped his face and hands clean on his napkin and gave a little sigh—half contentment, half regret at not getting to finish the cake.

"Right, yes," he began, fiddling nervously with his glasses. "I've been through the records that His Majesty brought back from...Flobovia." He forced the name out with a bit of pained disbelief that any country could have such a ridiculous appellation. "I think we're going to have some difficulty," he continued nervously.

It was at that moment that the Calm Emotions spell ran out and the panic stabbed for me again with pointy, razor-sharp claws. The claws stabbed into the nice warm fog of happiness that I was basking in, got lost, and decided to go grab a drink instead of bothering with me. I smiled loosely and settled back, feeling a little disconnected from everything, in a pleasantly hazy sort of way.

"The problem," Einarr continued. "Is that the Lady of Secrets is dead—at least, as far as we can tell. It's not a question of strengthening her with worship, we need to reinstantiate her consciousness, rebuild her ability to manifest. And that's going to require...sacrifice. A lot of sacrifice."

Everyone at the table shifted nervously.

"Are we talking human sacrifice...?" Thomas asked uncomfortably.

Einarr looked appalled and shook his head fiercely. "No no no! What would the goddess of knowledge and secrets want with death? No, that wouldn't do her any good at all. People need to sacrifice their secrets to her. Permanently."

We all stared at him in confusion.

He seemed frustrated by our lack of comprehension. "It's simple," he said impatiently. "We need to get as many people as possible to pick one—preferably more—of their secrets and sacrifice it to her. It has to be something that is actually a secret, and that benefits the person, either by staying a secret or by being revealed. For example, a chef might give up the secret recipe to his most popular dish. A young man might give up the fact that he is in love with a particular girl but hasn't had the nerve to tell her. A general might give up a secret attack plan." His eyes slid to me. "A man from another world might give up his memory of his wife, or his children."

He paused to study each of us in turn; we all looked nervous and uncomfortable, even Thomas. "It isn't a hard process. You simply write your secret down and drop it into a fire that burns atop her altar, offering a short and simple prayer. Something like 'Lady of Secrets, take my secret unto thee.' The paper burns and the secret is gone, giving her its strength. The more important the secret, the better."

I frowned blurrily. "Whut d'you mean, 'the secret is gone'? How can a secret be gone?"

"She will take it unto Herself. The person making the sacrifice will no longer remember it; I can't tell if they will even remember what the secret was about, or if they'll even remember making the sacrifice. She might render the act of sacrifice itself a secret."

"If they need to be significant secrets, then this could be a problem," Albrecht mused. "But I suppose I could write it down and review the copy afterwards...?"

Einarr was shaking his head firmly. "No. That's the point—it's a sacrifice. If you have a way to recover the secret then it isn't a sacrifice. That would probably actually hurt her instead of helping. And we'll only get one shot at this; a divine resurrection is a onetime thing. Right now, the last fragments of her being are drifting around the multiverse on the aetheric winds. This ritual will pull them all together and allow them to coalesce back into a conscious entity...but the pull that brings them together will also shred them; if there is insufficient power to bind them together, then they will disperse again, but this time in such tiny fragments that they could never be restored."

We all sat quietly, digesting that.

"So, loss of secrets. Important secres. And we have to make sure that the secrets aren't recoverable afterwards," I mused. "Will, at least this can be usefl. If there's anything that we want to be sure the enemy can't find out, we c'n sacfice that knowledge. Fr'example, the current location of Grǫfhamr; have one mage choose the next location and take it there, then sacrifice the knowledge of where it is. The next time we muv, we take care to make sure that the mage doin' th' muv doesn't know where he's teleporting from."

"With respect, Your Majesty," Einarr said, in that exact tone that conveys no respect at all. "It doesn't work like that. If you benefit from the knowledge being destroyed, then it isn't a sacrifice. The important part is the sacrifice, the choice to give up personal advantage to aid the Lady."

I sighed and took another hit of the Scotch to lubricate my thoughts. "Well tha' sucks. Would've been nice if it coulda been useful, at least." I fell to muttering about the obnoxiousness of divinity and how, even when they actually existed, they were nothing but trouble and how much better off we would be without them.

Thomas was eying me with concern. "Are you all right, Your Majesty? You may have had a little too much—"

I took another belt on my Scotch and smiled at him, a bit blearily. "'m fine. Silly Thomas, 'm jus' fine. Just thinking how to get everyone to," I paused to organize my thoughts, which had suddenly become slippery and hard to keep track of. "How to get everyone to sacfeesh...sacarfice...give up their secres, 'n' make sure no'ne cheash." I took another shot of Scotch and waved my finger in the air to emphasize the great importance of my thinking. "It is impor-tant," I declaimed, taking care to form the syllables correctly, since for some reason I seemed to have developed a speech impediment. I paused to ponder that for a moment, allowing the marvelous might of my magically modified mental machinery to macerate the conception completely to create clarity. "Ha," I laughed, smiling to myself. "Allirashon'sh cool. Loves me some allirashun. Mental mashinry mazratin', ha. S'good."

Albrecht, Einarr, Thomas, Duncan, Robert, Rob, Bob, Aerith, and Suze watched as I slid messily off my chair and collapsed, hugging my lovely bottle to my chest like a teddy bear. They were quite bemused as I started snoring and drooling on myself.
