Down the canyon, Icefi was busily drawing a circle in the dirt, taking great care to make it as perfect as possible. The rest of us stood back by the door to Grǫfhamr, heads together in a quick huddle.

"Right, everyone got both your marbles?" I asked for the fifth time. The Landguard kept a professionally stoic face—all except for Duncan, whom I had flat ordered Thomas to bring along. He glared at me, and I could almost hear his thoughts: stop wittering like a mother hen and get on with it. All of the non-Landguard just rolled their eyes and pulled back their sleeves to show the marble Sovereign Glued to their left forearm and the one held carefully in their hand.

"Yes, Jake, we remember," Unn said with obviously forced patience. "Keep hold of the second marble. Don't drop it accidentally or we all get teleported back here. If there's any danger or you even think there might be, drop your marble and we'll all be yanked back here. Be sure that the one on your arm doesn't get removed or you won't be touching a marble when you drop one and so you won't be teleported. If the marbles fail, activate the shirt button and go ethereal. If that fails, activate the left toe ring to Plane Shift to the Astral, then activate the right toering to Greater Planeshift back to the Prime Material. We know."

"Unn, you forgot the toothcaps!" Drifa admonished her. "Remember, don't eat anything while we're there, because if you bite down too hard on the right side it will Greater Teleport you home, as long as you're on the Prime Material and not in an extradimensional space."

Albrecht nodded with unconvincing solemnity. "And you absolutely must remember your belt buckle—it's a single-use Shapechange spell; just twist it clockwise to activate it. Your best bet is probably to turn into a shadow or a wraith so that you can blend in."

"Yes," Reynard answered with equally false seriousness. "Failing that, however, chronotyryns are a good choice for both combat and escape because they have two actions per round. And, of course, chronotyryns look like—"

"—a bird twelve feet from beak to tail," Thomas picked it up without a pause. "It has a raptor-like beak and arms sprouting from under its wings. The overall color is—"

"—that of adamantine, because that is what the feathers are composed of," Drifa continued again, nodding sagely. "Of course, there are other good choices aside from chronotyryns, such as trumpet archons, which have a permanent—"

"—magic circle against evil which will hedge out most of our opponents. More importantly though, they have at-will Greater Teleport which should let you escape from almost anything, unless you find yourself in a Dimensional Lock, which is a good time to use the Dispel Magic spell that's in the green charm on your bracelet; just name your target, then break the charm off."

I grimaced and rubbed my neck in embarrassment. "Right, sorry. I may have repeated all that a bit much." Every single one of the others gave me a speaking look that practically shouted ya think?! I raised my hands placatingly. "Ok, ok, definitely repeated it too many times. Now, here's the plan." At my gesture, Suze produced a sheet of carefully-folded paper from her Belt of Many Pouches and passed it around. Everyone was very careful to open it only the tiniest bit to make it as hard as possible for anyone scrying us to see what was written on it.

Isaac read the paper, passed it to Unn, and then stared at me in squint-eyed distrust. "What does that mean?" he demanded suspiciously.

I shrugged. "I promise, it won't harm any of us. And, even if it all goes wildly wrong, I'll be standing right next to you. Whatever happens, I'll be affected just as much as you."

He glared at me for another long moment, then hmphed in disgust and let it go.

"Everyone got it?" I asked, looking around the circle. I got a bunch of puzzled frowns back, but they all nodded. They didn't understand why I was asking for this, but at least they understood what I was asking for.

I glanced over my shoulder; Icefi had finished scattering amber dust in a careful layer across the area of the circle and was standing on the edge of it, chanting with skeletal hands upraised. The circle was beginning to glow a soft blue.

"Time to go," I said, straightening up with a soft sigh and a knot in my stomach. It was time for the Rousing Speech. I'd put it together last night and was proud of it. It was a mosaic of the best and brightest, the most inspiring battle addresses of my world: "we band of brothers" and "this, our finest hour," and "Death said that he and his were going to waltz through Grǫfhamr, and we choked him with those words. We have done the impossible, and that makes us mighty. Today is our chance...and he won't see this coming."

I was ready to give the speech, to inspire them...and suddenly the reality of my situation hit me. We were about to go walk into the arms of the oldest lich in the world and try to do a deal with him so that he would get us an audience with Death so that we could get rid of him...or have him kill us, one or the other. My throat locked in a knot of near-terror. I couldn't make a sound; I just stood there with my mouth open while they all looked at me, waiting to hear what I'd started to say. I could feel my uncertainty starting to spread to them as they waited...and waited...and waited.

I closed my eyes and took a deep breath, reaching for that armor of mental ice that had carried me through so many crises in this world and in my own...and it wouldn't come. My failure to find it made my stomach knot up even worse as panic washed in; for a moment I teetered right on the edge of losing it completely. Jesus Christ, what was I doing? Every god I had interacted with had crushed me utterly; even Loki, one of the lesser gods, had left me quaking in helpless terror with a simple glance. How the hell did I think I could take on the literal God of Death???

I grabbed my fear and twisted it, turning it into anger at myself. Rage flooded through me—how dared I panic at this moment? Everyone else was fine, what was my problem? Why was I special, what did I get to hold up a stress card right now? The rage leaped higher; it swept the fear aside like a blowtorch burning away cobwebs. I felt my spine straighten and my face firm up as fire roared across my soul. I let it wash through me, focusing it like a laser on my enemies: death and its embodiment. The loss of human lives and all the joyous potential enshrined within them. The mockery of life into which Neklos—that infuriating, psychotic, megalomaniac, childish Invisble Sky Daddy—had twisted those bright sparks. The deaths of countless civilization that He had caused, over and over, across multiple eons. Most of all, I let myself rage against the stupidity and selfishness of the gods and their insane petty politics that inflicted such horror upon those who should be their charges. My humility, my uncertainty, my fear, my belief that some obstacles could not be overcome, my desire to be liked instead of obeyed—I crushed them to the earth and ground them beneath my heel. My eyes snapped open.

"Let's go kick some ass," I told them with a bared-teeth expression that bore no relation to a smile.

Apparently I wasn't the only one feeling a need to play mother hen, because Icefi was repeating everything he'd told us last night, looking around the circle to ensure that each of us had understood and acknowledged.

"Again, I can't go with you," he told us. "The Creator ordered me and the rest of the Free Corps to never come within a mile of him, so you'll need to make your own way. Remember to hold your hands up and away from your body as you pass through the Circle. When you arrive, you will be in the Creator's receiving room. It's heavily defended, and the defenders will attack immediately if they see anything they interpret as even possibly hostile. Someone will step forward to ensure that you aren't carrying any weapons. Don't move until they say you can or you'll be attacked." He paused, glancing at the Landguard's empty sheaths. "You aren't carrying any weapons, right?" he asked, suspicion clearly evident in his sepulchral voice.

Duncan grunted, shooting me a disgusted look. He had been delighted and grateful to be put back on active duty...and promptly infuriated when I told him that he needed to disarm. It had actually been pretty funny—once he finally accepted the order, he handed over both his swords and a dagger from his sleeve. Thomas had cleared his throat significantly; Duncan had looked at him in betrayal and pulled a knife from each boot and one from a sheathe on his back in what I liked to think of as 'Yippee-kai-ay' position. A raised eyebrow from his Commander got a pair of brass knuckles added to the pile...and two daggers from the small of his back...and a thin-bladed knife hidden under his bracer...and an adamantine garrote that was stitched to the inside of his belt...and a ring that sprouted a small needle when the stone was twisted...and—well, suffice it to say that it took a little while for him to disarm.

"Yes, we all remember this," Thomas said patiently. "Yes, we have all disarmed." He gestured to the empty sheaths on his, Duncan, and Alpha Squad's hips.

Icefi looked at all of us one last time, then nodded his skull. "Teleportation Circle," he declared, in a voice that made reality twist around us. The blue-glowing circle on the ground flared for a moment, then faded as the spell was completed.

"Take your places," he said, waving us all to the perimeter of the circle.

I moved to my position between Thomas and Duncan, only to feel a slight bump against my elbow. Turning, I saw Suze squeezing in on my left.

"Absolutely not," I told her firmly. "Get back inside."

She gave me a mulish look, folding her arms across her chest and scowling. "I am the Imperial Executive Assistant, Order of the Golden Pen! It's my job to be beside you so that I can assist!"

"No. Way." I shook my head. "Get back to the city and get out of sight. This is no place for a noncombatant. Shoot, I wouldn't be going if there was an option."

She was trying to look firm, but I could see 'scared' and 'upset' fighting their way to the surface. "But—"

"Go back to the city, Suze," Thomas told her firmly. "You are of the Land, and I will not allow you to be harmed. If you were to come, you would only be a distraction."

"But—" she tried to object. Finally she slumped in defeat. "Fine," she grumbled, stepping back from the circle. "I'll just stay here, worrying that you're all going to die and I'm going to have to wait, not knowing, until we all get eaten by monsters and turned into undead horrors."

I blinked and grinned despite myself. "Wow! Someone took twenty ranks in Perform (Guilt Trip)!"

She shot me a sour look and sniffed, trying to look offended. She couldn't hold the look for more than a moment before her expression softened into worry. "Come back, M'Lord. The Land needs you."

The fury-born confidence had mostly faded now, but from somewhere I mustered up a cocky smile and a jaunty salute. "That's the plan, darlin'," I told her. "Tell the kitchen to do roast beef for lunch—we'll be back before it's ready to eat."

She raised her hand in a tiny little wave; I winked at her and stepped through the portal.

One stride forward—a sense of stretching, of my legs lengthening as though I wore thousand-league boots—and I was standing in a stone room the size of a soccer field, the ceiling towering forty feet overhead. Before my eyes could adjust to the dim, shadowy light, I felt Duncan, Thomas, and Alpha Squad slot into place around me. They were disarmed and had their hands raised, but they were still interposing their armored bodies between me and any possible threat.

I nearly fell over as a soft, feminine weight stumbled against my back.

"Goddamnit, Suze—!"

"Arrivals will be still!" came a hundred voices, all speaking in perfect unison. From the dimness around us floated five score Afis, several hundred floating black-metal golems with long arms ending in wicked claws...and more undead shadows than I could count.

"Well, ain't that a thing," Duncan muttered.

o-o-o-o

The inspection was minute; they verified that there were no weapons on any of us. They found the marbles concealed in our hands and pockets; the ones we had held concealed in our hands and our pockets were found and removed. The ones Sovereign Glued to our arms were cut loose—they allowed the Landguard to heal us afterwards. They found the Shirt Buttons of Etherealness that had been our backup escape plan. They found everything, including the magic toothcaps and the invisible enchanted toe rings we were all wearing, both of which were equipped with Mystic Aura spells so they didn't show up to Detect Magic.

How did they find all our most carefully hidden items, you might ask? Simple.

"Mindscrape," said a dozen of the Ice Assassins as they took up positions around us. My memories and those of all my companions were laid bare before them. Our plans, our clever preparations, our every secret spilled into their hands like the jackpot from a slot machine. They saw my arrival in Flobovia, they saw my scientific knowledge, they saw the nature and methods of orbital bombardment, they saw all the details of how to free Afi from the godshard. They saw the existence of the marbles and all of the other items. They saw the Belt of Many Pockets that Suze wore below the non-magical pouch at her hip. They saw the multitude of shrunken-holy-water-filled Portable Holes that each of had hidden around our persons, disguised as simple clothing. They even saw the key that Loki had given me which granted access to the invisible, undetectable-because-god, anti-magical extradimensional emergency bunker where several hundred Landguard waited alongside piles of all Thomas and the others' weapons and enough healing potions, wands, scrolls, staves, and other gear to fight a medium-sized war. They saw all of that, and they stripped it all away, passing it off to some of their number to be carried away to safe storage well away from us.

As if that weren't enough, the Icefi clones dumped eighty or ninety Mage's Disjunction spells on us until it was virtually certain that any magic items they could conceivably have missed had been rendered thoroughly mundane.

It wasn't just our possessions that they took or destroyed. They saw all the exploitable details of our relationships—my lingering fury at how Isaac and Reynard had edited my memories, my simultaneous respect for Thomas and fear of the violence he was capable of, the dislike and borderline contempt that I bore for Colborn...even my exasperated-but-slightly-proud anger at Suze for sneaking along with us instead of staying put at Grǫfhamr like I'd goddamn well told her to.

I tried to at least conceal our go-to-hell plan—Scrying to locate us, followed by orbital bombardment with antimatter osmium penetrators—but the spell brushed my efforts aside with contemptuous ease and ripped the details out of my agonized brain.

Once they were confident that we had been rendered as harmless as possible, we were hustled along a ridiculously wide corridor covered in elegant tapestries and statuary. Our escorts were carefully interleaved among us—each of us walked in a box consisting of one of Afi's Ice Assassins to left and right, a pair of those shadowy floating metallic constructs in front and behind, and a round dozen Greater Shadows filling in the corners. It was abundantly clear that we were prisoners, not guests, and that if any of us tried anything that our captors didn't like, we'd be dead before we could blink.

By the time we were in sight of the portal that lead to Afi's throne room, I was thoroughly disheartened. I'd brainstormed a lot of different defenses, and thought I'd covered the bases.I was frantically scrabbling around in the back of my head, looking for some sort of leverage to make this end in anything but a Total Party Kill...forget trying to win, at this point, I just wanted to escape. I glanced around at my companions, hoping for some sort of inspiration.

Thomas was the kind of person who got calmer as things got worse; by the time we walked through the archway into the throne room, I suspect his heart rate was somewhere around forty beats a minute. I couldn't do that.

Duncan, on the other hand, was fulminating. I couldn't do that.

Suze was terrified. I could absolutely do that.

The archway leading to Afi's throne room was filled with a shimmering curtain of rippling light that just screamed 'extradimensional portal.' My heart sank when I saw it—so much for the idea of the Grǫfhamrites being able to Scry us so they could rescue us...or even target the area for orbital bombardment. It had been a faint hope, but at least a hope.

We transitioned through the portal and I felt my heart quail; the room was designed to impress and oh boy did it succeed. As long as a soccer field, a hundred feet wide, eighty feet high, giant buttresses like ribs soaring across the span of the roof. The floor was a perfectly flat plane of perfectly hexagonal tiles the size of a dinner plate, each so precisely identical that they fit together with hardly a space between them. They came in subtly different shades of grey, lighter by the door and sweeping in a darkening wave that dragged the eye forcibly towards the dias at the end where Afi lounged upon his throne.

That room would have been intimidating enough empty, but it wasn't empty. The walls were lined with rank upon rank of midnight-skinned drow, rocky svirfneblin, pebble-skinned troglodytes, and creatures I'd never even heard of. Thousands of their flat, cold eyes turned to see us escorted up the room. Above their heads, looking down with watchful threat, floated scores of Icefis and even more of those floating golems.

Our escorts spread out, allowing us to move together while they formed a perimeter between us and the creatures on the walls. We advanced in an open box—our monstrous captors to the sides and behind. I found myself stumbling slightly, my knees getting a little wobbly, and my head turning back and forth as I looked around in growing desperation for anything that could be exploited for an advantage.

Thomas and Duncan closed ranks to my left and right. Alpha Squad took up position in a trailing wedge with us at the point, herding Suze and the others into the middle. Every Landguard stepped off on the left foot, their hobnailed boots slamming into the paving stones with a crash that managed to be intimidating even in these conditions. In perfect synch they led us up the aisle; instinctively, all of us fell into step. Bound together by the unstoppable certainty of their tread, buoyed up by the aura of courage and hope that they pushed out from behind implacable expressions, we went up the room as a disciplined force, not as a stumbling mass of frightened prisoners.

We reached the dias together and stopped with a final crashing step that echoed in the sudden silence.

Afi gazed down at us from above like a god in judgement, allowing the moment to linger until the tension could be cut with a knife. Wrapped in the aura of courage that flowed from my paladins, I was left untouched by what should have been a frightening moment. Instead, I stood serene, my mind ticking through possibilities with efficient speed. Force was out; only diplomacy and strategy remained, and I intended to wring every possible advantage from them. Everything I knew about Afi spread itself out in a neat grid in my mind, every fact and speculation fitting into a neat web as I searched for the lever that would let me move him to the path I wanted...the path where we all ended up alive and Neklos ended up dead.

After at least a minute of ominous, looming silence, Afi spoke.

"Prismatic Wall."

A brightly shimmering rainbow wall leaped into existence fifty feet behind us, stretching from wall to wall and floor to ceiling. Sight and sound of the room beyond the wall was shut off like flipping a switch. Isaac raised an eyebrow at that and Unn blinked in surprise. Reynard looked somewhere between impressed and awed.

Afi got to his feet and stared down at us.

"So. My Ice Assassins told me your plan to separate me from the godshard, and your idea for how to deal with Death. Explain to me why I need you now."
