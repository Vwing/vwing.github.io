"Thomas, don't do this! I order you not to do this!" I hissed. The words were coming directly from my autonomic system; my cerebral cortex knew perfectly well that it wasn't going to make a difference.

I was right, it didn't.

"We cannot deal with an Evil creature of that power level and intellect who has taken all of your extraworldly knowledge. "And we can verify that...Zone of Truth, Detect Thoughts, lots of ways. Besides, chances are very good that all of these things are Lawful Evil—Afi was an emperor for hundreds of years; that's not the act of a Chaotic being. That set me back. Thomas was absolutely right, this situation was completely beyond control. The only solution was to make sure that the Ice Assassins regarded us as useful allies, be ready to kill them all as soon as possible and hopefully before they killed us, and strike hard and fast against Afi himself.

Hm. Actually, we didn't need to strike against Afi, did we? He didn't even want to be fighting. We needed to strike at the root of the problem, Neklos. We could either depower him enough so the good gods could smoosh him, or we could power up the good gods—either way would work. Unless...was there a way to strike directly at Neklos, instead of going after his worshippers? Something was tugging at the back of my brain, but I couldn't see it yet.

After a minute I shook my head to get rid of the stray thought. It would surface or it wouldn't, but I had other things to do right now.

"Ok, Thomas is right, we really can't contain this information," I said to the table at large. "Only thing I can think to do is step it up, just blitzkrieg Af—Lichboy out of existence before he or his little muppets can use all that info against us. If we can take him out and depower Deadboy enough, maybe the gods will squish any remaining Icies for us. Anyone got a better idea?"

We all stared at each around the table for several long seconds. Eventually the silence was broken by Thomas, speaking over the telepathic link.

*Your time is up, monster. I am sending the messages now.* He raised his gauntlet and tapped the stud to create the ghost sparrow.

Before he could give it the message, Icefi called over the link. *All right, all right! I'm in the canyon now, putting on the helmet!* There was a momentary pause, and then it 'spoke' again. *There. I'm Chaotic Good now. Stop the messages.*

Thomas spoke to his patiently-waiting ghost sparrow. "Sending to Healer Specialist Elaine Archer: confirm Ice Assassin alignment change." The sparrow vanished with a pop! There was a pregnant pause as we all waited; a few seconds later, another sparrow appeared.

"Change confirmed: lich is Chaotic Good," the sparrow said in a no-nonsense soprano.

Thomas nodded to himself, then lifted his gauntlet again. "Sending to Guardian First Kara Carter. Confirm Elaine clear."

The response was back in moments. "Confirmed. Lich did not speak or look in Elaine's direction."

I cocked my head in puzzlement. "What was that?"

"Elaine was near the Helm to cast Detect Chaos and Detect Good," he told me. "Kara's job was to stay well back with a telescope and make sure that the thing didn't just Mindscrape Elaine into believing she'd verified its alignment."

We all digested that.

"This spell is really going to make everything suck, isn't it?" I asked fatalistically.

Thomas shrugged. "The Landguard spends a lot of time thinking about how to deal with mind magic. Welcome to our world," he said sardonically.

I sighed, dismissed the meeting, and went to bed.

"As to containing Hammond, you take some fenfrogs in with you and they lick the magic out of him. The maze is the easy part; you just use the right hand rule. Put your right hand on the wall and don't let go as you walk through. Assuming it's a one level maze, you'll pass through every part of it and eventually find the exit."

Everyone's eyes flicked up to the ceiling and their fingers twiddled a bit as they thought their way through that one. Slow nods and expressions of surprise showed as they all got it.

"Now, as to how we get you through the maze quickly enough to surprise Hammond...that one is going to be hilarious. I just hope you guys like each other a lot."

That got me a suspicious look, which I happily ignored while giggling maniacally to myself. I wasn't about to satisfy their curiosity just yet. The whole 'dramatic reveal' thing was just too much fun...by which, of course, I really meant 'too satisfying to my ego.'

Hey, we all have our faults, ok?

I quickly shifted the topic before they could press for details. "Before we get into that, though, there's something we need to take care of."

o-o-o-o

"Arros, God of Magic, Lord of Wizards, Supreme Archmage and Master of Mystics, Grand High Poobah of Magicdom, Coolest Banana of the Mystically Badass Bunch, please grant us the tremendous honor of your presence!" I called out, pouring a few drops of Cire Perdue on the hastily improvised altar to His Magical Mightiness while carefully holding my nose so as to avoid adding vomit to the offering. I wasn't too clear on this whole 'talking to gods' thing, but I was pretty sure that vomit was an unwelcome offering.

Standing behind me, Drifa looked absolutely horrified at my departure from scripture. So did Isaac and Reynard and...ok, basically everyone. Except Allison, of course. She was snickering fit to beat the band.

"Well that was unusual," Arros said with a smile. "Don't think I've ever heard some of those epithets before. Not sure how I feel about being called a fruit."

I strangled a laugh at that one; I was pretty sure he didn't know the idiom, and I certainly wasn't about to explain it to him.

"Thanks for coming, good to see you again," I told him, striving for 'casual yet respectful.' I think I probably got something more like 'inappropriately chummy' but at least His Magicness hadn't obliterated me in a flash of divine temper, so I was going to call it a win.

"I was just sitting down to lunch; would you care for a bite?" Arros asked, producing a full high tea from some extradimensional hyperspatial tesseract pocket that I wasn't going to think about because I was so tired of this world's casual physics breaking. (And why that was the thing I had trouble with, given that I was busy having a conversation with a physical god, I don't know.)

"That would be lovely, thanks," I said, sitting down very gingerly in the chair that he conjured for me. It looked like a giant soap bubble and, as I sat on it, it conformed perfectly to my body. I had a sneaking suspicion that Arros had been hanging out with Loki lately, because this seemed much more the Trickster's brand of wacky fun.

Arros lifted up the cover of a serving dish to display a perfectly-arranged plate: soft boiled egg in the half shell in the center, surrounded by butter-soaked toast, sauteed mushrooms, and fried tomatoes. He set it carefully in front of me and then took one of his own.

I felt amazingly uncomfortable eating while the Landguard and the rest stood behind me watching, but I wasn't about to poke my host about it; I was here to ask a favor, and implying that someone was being rude is not conducive to favor-getting.

Fortunately, Arros was actually quite mannerly. Once he and I were served, he looked up at the others and gestured for them to sit. The table obligingly expanded to make room, and new chairs popped out of nowhere. "Well?" he demanded. "Aren't you going to join us?"

Drifa, Unn, Isaac, Reynard, and Albrecht sat promptly. Colborn was a bit more hesitant about sitting at a breakfast table when he couldn't eat, but he did take a seat. Thomas gave Alpha Squad a speaking look and then joined us; the four of them stayed on their feet.

Arros raised an eyebrow in amusement. "Don't worry, Commander. I guarantee the safety of everyone here. Your men may feel free to have some lunch."

They were clearly reluctant, but they did sit down and allow themselves to be served. Just as well; it's not every day that the God of Magic serves you an English breakfast for lunch with his own hands while you're sitting in the middle of a magically created mansion in the middle of a city made of Portable Holes. It would be a hella story to tell the grandkids.

"So, what can I do for you, Jake?" Arros inquired, dunking strips of toast in his egg and nibbling on them daintily.

"It's about the Edict, actually," I said, forking up a couple of the mushrooms. (Unsurprisingly, they were simply divine.) "We're in a war for survival here and, let's face it, we're heavily outmatched. I need to be able to munchkin without crossing any lines that are going to get me smacked, and right now I don't know where those lines are. Can I get a copy of this thing?"

Arros hmm'd and chewed his mushrooms quietly. He paused, conjured a glass of fresh-squeezed orange juice (one appeared in front of each of us) and sipped it thoughtfully.

"The Edict is a bit...fluid," he said slowly. "It boils down to 'don't do anything that might let you ascend,' but the list of things that fit under that umbrella is pretty broad, and it gets longer every time you mortals come up with some new overly creative idea."

I sighed in exasperation, and paused to step on my temper. Showing how irritated I was by this was not conducive to getting what I wanted. "I can understand that," I said, nodding soberly. "Would it be good enough if I promised that I have not the slightest interest in ascending and, even if I did find a way to do it, I wouldn't use it? Seriously, all I want to do is win this war and avoid dying until I can go home."

Arros flicked a glance at Thomas and the Archmagi, pursing his lips. After a moment he gave a tiny shrug and shook his head slightly as though discarding a thought.

"Not really," he said. "Even if you didn't use an idea to ascend, someone else would. We tried doing the case-by-case thing for a few hundred years and it worked poorly, so now we've got a zero-tolerance policy." He paused for another bite of toast. "That said, whoever responds to the issue has wide latitude on how they deal with it. The first time you broke the Edict, you did it in Ilara's temple, so she was the one who responded. She's a pretty serious person, so she tends to take a fairly hard-line stance.

"On the other hand, when you started buying huge numbers of spells, that was in my portfolio, so I was the one who responded. Given that it was a second offense, I had intended to erase you from existence, but you made me laugh so I decided to give you a warning instead." He delicately sliced a tomato in half and took a bite.

I shivered at the casual declaration of how close I'd been to being annihilated. Let's hear it for temper tantrums that happened to tickle the divine funnybone! My hands felt clammy and were shaking, so I set my silverware down and busied myself with my napkin for a moment.

"Tea?" asked Arros, casually extending the pot with a friendly smile that chilled me straight to the bone. I nodded and pushed my cup forward, keeping my hand in contact with the table the whole time to hide my shakes.

"Anyway," Arros said, still in that casually relaxed tone that was now far more disturbing than outright threats. "It is a bother, getting pulled away from what I'm doing to come down here and shred the soul of some stupid mortal with poor judgement. So, I'll tell you what: I'll leave you a fragment of Myself. You can consult with It before you do something that you think might be a problem. If It says no, or even seems dubious, don't do whatever it is." His looked up from his tea and met my gaze; his smile remained friendly, but it didn't reach his eyes.

"If you do disregard the advice of the fragment...well, let's just say that all of us are tired of dealing with you on these issues."

My entire side of the table was frozen. Even the Landguard, their paladinhood normally making them immune to fear effects, were motionless and pale.

Arros swept his gaze up and down the table once, then released the sense of doom he was radiating. "More tea, anyone?" he asked brightly.

I waited until I could lift my cup to my lips without my hands shaking and sloshing it everywhere. Sip. Sip again. Set the cup gently and precisely back on the saucer. Deep breath.

"Just one question, if I may, please?" I asked hesitantly. I waited for Arros to give me a regal nod before I continued. "There won't be any ex post facto punishment, right? If we ask about something and your fragment says ok, you won't decide after the fact that what we did was too powerful and kill us all for it...right?"

Arros affected a hurt expression, one hand over his heart. "Why, Jake, how could you think I'd be so unreasonable?! No, of course not. I promise that neither I, nor any other god, will tear your soul to aetheric shreds and scatter the fragments into the Far Realms if you manage to exceed the expectations of my fragment." He paused, then gave me a thin smile. "Of course, I wouldn't suggest deliberately trying such a thing."

I blanched and had to take a moment to put myself back together. "Right, understood, will comply, message oh-so-very-much received." I paused to swallow and work some spit back into my suddenly bone dry mouth. "Uh...I guess, as long as you're here, I could ask you: we need to produce a fair amount of magical gear for our army...things like Fortifying Bedrolls, maybe Rings of Regeneration, magic swords like the Landguard have. Nothing crazy powerful and I think it's mostly on the regular item lists. Is it ok if I automate that process?"

Arros pursed his lips, eyeing me thoughtfully for a long moment. "You'd need to use Wishes for that, and lots of them. How do you intend to deal with the experience point requirements?"

I gulped. "I figured I'd use one Wish to create a battery that stores experience points. I'll only make one, and it won't be refillable—once it's empty, it's empty. It'll be able to allocate some of its contents into any spell engines that we create, or to provide them to a caster for purposes of crafting or spellcasting. Nothing else—it won't let people just level up. Is that ok?" I held my breath, hoping that this wasn't going to get me crushed like a bug.

He pondered, then shrugged acceptance. "I suppose so. As long as you don't start supplying magic items to outsiders, and you keep it to relatively straightforward items—I would suggest avoiding Rings of Wishes and Candles of Invocation, for example. But Fortifying Bedrolls and Rings of Regeneration aren't a problem. Those swords are fine as well, but only for the Landguard." He flicked a glance at Thomas. "And, Commander? Keep your intake process as it is. Don't start having one every week or lowering your standards just so that you can outfit everyone in Grǫfhamr with your gear."

Thomas nodded gravely. "Yes, Magelord, I understand. One request: the Landguard has suffered enormous losses recently, and we are in the process of using Ice Assassins to refill our ranks. The Writ restricts us to two thousand members, so we won't go beyond that. Is it permitted?"

Arros cocked his head, an amused expression on his face. "So, you're going to create a Landguard consisting of hundreds of copies of your existing members, all of whom are magical constructs that desperately want to destroy their originals and will be looking for any loophole in their orders in order to accomplish that goal?" He snickered. "In addition to being God of Magic, I am also the God of Cleverness and Lawyers. How can I say no to an idea as crazy as that? Only a paladin would be willing to take such a crazy risk. Feel free."

Thomas nodded his head in gratitude, but didn't speak. He was a lot braver than I was, and he had that whole 'immune to fear because paladin' thing going on, but I was pretty sure he was still almost as rattled as I was by Arros's casual discussion of soul-rending and Far-Realms-scattering.

Arros glanced around at the state of everyone's lunch. For the most part, we had all just nibbled, too focused on the rather terrifying discussion to eat very much, but we had all at least had enough not to offend the chef.

"Well, I must be off. I'll leave the table and food so you can all finish up. Oh, and here's that fragment that I promised." He opened his hand to reveal a tiny sun, no larger than my thumbnail but so bright that I couldn't look at it, and so hot I could feel the heat from across the table.

Arros flicked his fingers and the sun leaped across the table, straight into Reynard's open mouth. The Archmage body jerked into an agonized arch and he screamed. His skin flared red as though he were being burned from the inside, and golden light blasted from his eyes and throat. The seizure went on for long, long seconds before he suddenly relaxed, his face going calm and blank. His eyes blinked open to reveal only swirling multicolored light.

"I am here, Master," said my recently resurrected friend in a chorus of completely inhuman voices.

Arros gave a happy smile. "Excellent! Do try to keep them in line, won't you? Anyway, I'm off!"

He vanished in a puff of smoke, leaving us sitting in front of a lovely luncheon alongside an inhuman semi-divine monster who wore Reynard's skin like a cheap suit.
