The canyon floor was bedlam. Semi-organized bedlam, but still bedlam. No longer were people racing out of the various Holes on the south wall of the canyon, past the altars, and into the corresponding Hole on the north wall. Instead, they came out of the various Holes on the south wall, into the Teleportation Circle that had been set up in front of it, out the linked Circle on the top of the canyon where all our industry was set up, through the various POPE engines, made a quick stop at their assigned FLEA so they could go from being a level 1 commoner to a level 60 multi-caster, then through the appropriate Teleportation Circle that led back down to their particular altar, and into the corresponding Hole on the north wall. Everything was set up so that they never had to change direction or stop moving...if you could twist your head around far enough, the whole thing was a straight line path. It just happened to be a straight line that had Euclid spinning in his grave so fast you could have run a steam engine off of him.

As the new POPEs came online, we'd been setting up new FLEAs and new altars to work with them. Another (particularly involved) set of spell engines were creating the Teleportation Circles. It was a complex process; two engines fired, using True Creation to create two spidersilk blankets. A third fired, casting Teleportation Circle on one of the blankets, with the other blanket as the emergence point. Finally, a fourth engine cast Permanency on the Circle, at which point a railgun of people would snatch up the blankets and move them to where they were needed.

The blankets were, in my opinion, a very cool hack. The intent of the spell was clearly to be immobile, but the wording was that it could be cast on 'the floor or other horizontal surface,' which meant that blankets were perfectly doable. I'd been impressed the first time that Albrecht told me about this, back when we first met. Actually seeing them being set up was even more impressive. The upshot was that our mobility was increasing by leaps and bounds every round.

The flow of people was not as efficient as it could be, however. There was too much rubbernecking as people paused momentarily to gaze in awe at the Milky Way-esque spray of glowing haze that had settled over the altars. By now we had several dozen altars stretching down the canyon for a hundred yards; the haze stretched from one end of the line to the other, overhanging several dozen yards on each end. It had no defined shape, ebbing and flowing slowly in something that was either simple Brownian motion or a fibrillating heartbeat, I wasn't sure which.

The haze was...impressive. And scary. And disturbing. And a lot of other adjectives that I was a bit too numb to come up with at the moment. It was composed of the light emitted by individual star-like points of light in varying colors—black and silver and blue and green and white and gold. They varied in size; some were smaller than a grain of sand, one was the size of a cow, and most were about the size of a marble . There were a lot more black stars than all of the other colors together. Disturbingly many. And most of them were above average size. The implications of all this were sufficiently obvious, even without the fact that every sacrifice was now generating a colored star that floated up to join the rest.

"Well that's not good," I said with studied calm.

"I don't see any more black stars going up," Drifa said in the tone of someone desperately groping for a silver lining.

We all stared at it for a while in silence.

After a while, I noticed something odd: the stars at the southernmost end of the ravine were substantially larger than average, each being about the size of a plum. "What's that?" I asked, pointing.

"That's an experiment suggested by Archmage Cullen, M'Lord," Suze told us. "Everyone who graduates from the FLEA gains a very large number of skill points. Some of them are being told to sink all of those points into highly esoteric Language- and Knowledge Skills like 'clothing fashions in the merchants quarter of Akornhamr between 1000 BA and 1002 BA.' That gives them a lot of knowledge sufficiently esoteric that no one else in the world possesses it, which means it's effectively a secret. Then they sacrifice all of it."

I blinked at that. "That's...really slick," I said. "Isn't it not that big a sacrifice, though? Sounds like it's most unimportant trivia."

Albrecht shrugged. "They're basically giving up several hundred skill points that they could have used for things that actually benefited them. Based on the size of the stars we're getting, it seems efficient. It's been running since last night and the results seem positive, so we're going to switch everyone over to doing that. Probably not all of their skill points, but the majority of them." He paused. "Personally, I'm just glad that it turns out you can sacrifice broad swaths of knowledge at a time, without needing to write down each individual item."

I nodded in silent agreement, still watching the parade of lights before me. After a moment, a thought struck me and I turned to Suze. "How did you know about that, Suze?"

She ducked her head a bit but maintained eye contact. "I figured it was my job, Your Majesty, so I went around the city last night to see who was who and what was going on."

My eyebrows went up in pleased surprise. "Nice going," I told her. I was reminded again of what a treasure I had found in my memory maiden.

She blushed and smiled, ducking her head a bit more. "Thank you, Your Majesty."

I gave her a wink and then turned back to the others. "Ok, things seem to be coming along here as far as getting it done. We're two days in, maybe halfway through the city's population, and the black secrets still outnumber the rest. Nothing to do that I can think of aside from keeping on keeping on. Drifa, how much time do we have left? When is the ritual over?"

"When we say it is, Your Majesty," she responded. "There was a series of ritual elements that marked the beginning of the ritual, and there's another series that mark the end. When we perform those, the spell completes and the Lady's...form, I guess?...is finalized. We can keep going as long as we want, but once we finish it, that's it. No second chances."

Everyone looked thoughtful at that.

"How long does it take to do the closing ceremonies?" I asked.

"A couple of hours," she replied. "And there can't be any more sacrifices during that time, or it will destabilize everything."

"What signs will we receive that the spell is ready for a successful completion?" Isaac asked.

Drifa shrugged. "No idea," she said. She waved a hand at the haze above us. "I wasn't even expecting this. The whole ritual is spit and baling wire; nothing like it has been done in recorded history, so we have no clue what we'll see along the way."

"Hmph," Isaac muttered disapprovingly.

"Ok, so, I guess we're back to the 'nothing more to do except keep on keeping on.'" I said. "Given that, what's our next course of action? Anyone?"

*My suggestion would be to talk to Icefi about what Afi is up to, especially this research into Mindscrape,* Albrecht sent to all of us over our Telepathic Bond. "We should probably try to brainstorm up ways of locating the lichs' phylacteries," he said aloud.

I blinked at the two-tiered response, and then recognized his thought process: he was providing plausible intel for any gods who might be scrying us while we were exposed outside the city, making it seem that we weren't aware of the possibility of divine scrying.

*I'm with you on that. Hey, Icefi! What can you tell us about what Afi's up to? Especially with this Mindscrape research?* I sent. "Seems sensible," I said, responding to Albrecht's out-loud comment. I was having trouble switching between the two conversations; I suspect my words came out pretty wooden, but hopefully it wouldn't be too obvious. "Before we do that though, could we take a break? I don't know about you guys, but I could use some quiet time to think." By which I actually meant I didn't want to have to juggle two conversations at once.

*There is a limit to what I can tell you,* Icefi responded mentally.

"Of course," Albrecht said, answering my out-loud words. "Why don't you head up to the top of the canyon and walk around a bit? I'm going to take a look at the production of the new altars, see if there's any way to streamline that." Giving a polite nod, he walked off and through one of the Teleportation Circles that lead up to the canyon top.

*Why so?* I sent over the link in response to Icefi's rather cagey answer about information sharing.

*I may be Chaotic Good now,* the thing responded. *That doesn't mean that I can go against my programming. I still need to do what I view as in my creator's best interests, it's just that now I have a different perspective on what those interests are.*

I noticed Suze looking worried by that response and forced myself to keep a blank, thoughtful expression as we walked through the Circle to the top of the canyon. Choosing a direction at random, I started pacing away from the canyon edge, the Landguard spreading out around me in their standard overlapping circles of protection. Alpha Squad and Thomas stayed close in, but another thirty or forty Landguard formed several outer perimeters. By now, the various production lines had spread far enough, and there were enough commoner railguns moving goods around, that we had to wind around quite a bit to keep to open ground, and it made it hard for the Landguard to hold relative position. Somehow, they managed.

~Can't really call these 'commoner' railguns any more,~ I thought to myself. ~These are now some of the most powerful people in the world, or about to be.~ I shook the thought aside and concentrated on the mental conversation with the mega-lich Ice Assassin.

*Very well,* Albrecht sent. *What can you tell us?*

*As to the Mindscrape research, that's not the Creator,* Icefi told us with an edge of amusement. *That's actually us, members of the the Free Corps. We've been masquerading as the Creator and buying up all those scrolls and spellbooks.*

Pretty much everyone in the link sent the equivalent of a mental 'Huh?' at that.

*Aren't you worried about the potential consequences?* Albrecht sent. *If someone mentions the purchases in the presence of the real Afi he'll know that he hasn't been doing it, and that he never told any of his bound Ice Assassins to do it. If Neklos were manifesting at that time, it could put Him on the track to being aware of your 'Free Corps,' as you call it.*

A mental snort came over the wire. *Hardly. Give us credit, mortal. Most of us have only existed for a few years, but we have millenia of experience to draw upon. We Mindscraped one of the Known into thinking that it was his idea, and that he was going to use it to research a mass version of Mindscrape that could be used as a battlefield spell. We purchase the books, copy them, then pass them on to him. He thinks that we're just other Known that he's delegated to.*

A mass version of Mindscrape? Now there was a charming thought. It was bad enough that Afi had a few legions of fanatically devoted clones of himself, all of whom had enough Diplomacy ranks to function as mind control. The idea of all of them being able to instantaneously edit the loyalties and memories of large numbers of opponents gave me a sick feeling in the pit of my stomach.

*I take it that's not what you're actually working on?* Unn asked.

*No. It would have some utility, of course, but it's not essential. No, we're looking for a way to remove the shard from the Creator.*

I stumbled in shock. *How does Mindscrape do that? The shard isn't just a memory, is it?* I demanded.

*The shard has both a mental and spiritual component,* Icefi responded. *Mindscrape leaves the mind utterly open and manipulable by the caster. It also has a limited access to the spiritual side of the target, which is what's used when you Mindscrape a target to have another alignment. We are trying to extend the spiritual side of it enough that we could edit out the shard.*

*Is that possible?* Drifa demanded, somewhere between awe and horror.

The response came tagged with a mental shrug. *Maybe not. Certainly we've had no success so far, but it's the only idea we've come up with for how to get the Creator free. If he retains the shard then, sooner or later, Neklos will take him over completely.*

*Couldn't happen soon enough for me,* Thomas sent over a private channel to me. *Neklos is a micromanager with no patience and, as far as I can tell, little experience in war. Afi is a general and battlemage with thousands of years of experience, strategically and tactically brilliant, and has a virtual hivemind of duplicates. I would much prefer that Neklos were in charge of the opposition.*

I nodded abstractedly at that. *Hey, why did Afi accept the shard in the first place?* I asked on the public 'frequency.' *If he's so smart, why didn't he recognize the danger?*

Icefi shrugged mentally. *I was created before he took the shard and I wasn't present when it happened, so I don't know the answer for certain. Neklos may not have given him a choice. Or perhaps the Creator felt he needed the power for something he was currently facing. Or perhaps Neklos influenced him...he's not able to simply force a sentient being to do something, but it does seem that he can exert subtle influence on the undead. As I said, I simply don't know.*

*Another question,* Unn put in. *You keep talking about how Afi Mindscraped himself, and how you Mindscraped one of the Known. Undead are immune to mind affecting spells, so how did you do that?*

*Simple,* the thing responded with overtones of amusement. *You Polymorph Any Object the target into something that isn't undead, then use Dispel Magic to put it back into its original form. As the Dispel goes off, you have a second caster do the Mindscrape as a readied action. The Mindscrape leaves the target with no memory of it being cast, and the two spells happen so close together that there's no perceptible time in between for the target to wonder why he's in a different form.*

*Clever,* Thomas sent. *As long as you're being clever, how did Afi find Magnhild's phylactery? It would be useful if we could do the same thing to the other Lich Kings. Or can't you tell us because it would threaten your creator?*

Mental snickering came over the link. *No, it's not an issue; you won't be able to use it. The Creator assigned a few thousand of us to each Lich King. First we made ourselves amulets of Mind Blank so we couldn't be detected via divinations, then we set up Permanent Telephatic Bonds connecting each member of the group to each other member and to the Creator. Finally, we used Polymorph Any Object to turn ourselves into dust mites—we cast it twice so that it was permanent. One of us would climb into the target's skull and wait there while the rest scattered around their lair and the surrounding area. After that it was just a question of waiting a few decades until the target went to check on their phylactery, then reporting back by way of the Telepathic Bond, passing on full details of the protections and how to bypass them.*

Well, that wasn't sobering at all. And the reason that the thing was willing to share was pretty obvious: even if we had the necessary legions of Ice Assassins and the time to wait, Afi probably never visited his own phylactery. He didn't need to, since he could just send a random Ice Assassin to do it, and it wasn't feasible to 'bug' every single Ice Assassin he had. Also, it was even more sobering to hear that there were "thousands" of Ice Assassins—actually, tens of thousands, since he'd assigned thousands to each of several dozen Lich Kings. And that was probably only part of his force. I had known it was foolish, but I had still been holding on to the hope that maybe the six hundred-ish that we'd killed had been the majority of his total.

We spent a minute or so chewing on all that, but couldn't really see where to go with it from there. Since no one had any more questions, and the conversation seemed more or less over, we all turned our feet back towards Grǫfhamr. Meeting up at the entrance, we spent some not-insignificant time pacing the halls and discussing what to do next. It took a while, but eventually we had a consensus.

"Loki, Loki, Loki," I recited, feeling sufficiently afraid of what I was about to get into that a Lovecraft reference seemed appropriate.

"You don't have to shout, you know," the Harlequin-painted Trickster said from his position floating face-down above us in the corridor where there was totally not enough room for him. "What's goin' on, kid? Kudos on the magic stuff, by the way. The production lines are slick."

"Thanks," I told him diffidently. I was seriously worried about how he was going to react to my next question—worried enough that I was having trouble talking around the lump in my throat.

"So, what's on your brain?" he asked, floating down to the floor with a pike and a half-twist. He proceeded to pace us down the corridor, walking on his hands.

"We're private, right?" I asked, still stalling.

"As a new Army recruit," he told me. He frowned, then flipped himself upright. "Hm. Maybe not the best analogy. Privates don't actually have any privacy...although their privates are supposed to, but that's a fairly private subject, even though a lot of privates seem to enjoy talking about their privates."

I let that one go right on by; it wasn't anything I wanted to get into.

"So, there's something I wanted to ask you. This isn't one of my questions, I just wanted to get your reaction," I told him.

One red-and-black-diamond-spangled eyebrow went up. "Fascinating," he said in a completely calm monotone. "Shoot."

I licked my lips nervously and took the plunge. "Suppose that Deathboy...weren't...um.. here, any more. What do you think would happen?"

A wide grin split the clown face; he flickered and suddenly there was a Stetson-wearing, leather-chaps-clad cowboy rolling along beside me with a bowlegged gait, spurs jingling with every step. "Wal now, pahdna, y'all shur ain't thinkin' small, now is ya?" There was another flicker and he was in a tracksuit and running shoes.

"We should probably pick it up a bit. We're going to want to be transitioning quickly," he informed me, breaking into a high-paced lope. I sped up to match him, but it was a pretty darn fast jog and he didn't talk for a while. I was winded within a minute, but forced myself to struggle on.

"Death is one of the major portfolios," Loki murmured, his mouth covered by a pixelated blur as he took care to spread the words across half a dozen corridor segments. "If the Death god were to disappear, there'd be a major power struggle as everyone rearranged. It would be mostly political though, meaning that it would be happening in the Meeting Hall over in Sigil"—he glanced at me and saw my lack of comprehension. "Sigil, the city at the center of the multiverse. There's a Meeting Hall there that the gods use when we all need to gather. Last time we were there was when we created Shailos's Edict. Anyway, we'd all gather there to sort things out. We'd leave our seconds—angels, whatever—to deal with answering prayers and granting spells while we were there, so there wouldn't be as much pressure being placed here on the Prime Material. It wouldn't make the wars just stop, but it would prevent them from ramping up any further, and the local reps might take some time to regroup instead of pushing forward. So, yeah, it would probably take some of the pressure off of you. Why?"

Now we got to the real sticking point. "Suppose that we were, um...maybe...possibly somehow tangentially involved in the Death God not being around anymore. What would happen to us?"

He snickered. "Well now, that is an impressive number of weasel words, isn't it? I think it would very much depend on how the new power structure shook out. If, after the shakeup, the majority of the top gods liked you, then you'd be fine. If not...well...squish."

"Ah," I said faintly, panting a bit and favoring the stitch in my side. "So...is there any...chance that you could...get us an audience with the Death God...do you think?" I leaned on my knees to catch my breath.
