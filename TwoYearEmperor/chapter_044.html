"You don't have barrels? You're kidding, right?" We were back in the War Room, once again alone, and I was busy being gobsmacked at the absence of a simple material object that meant my latest and greatest tactical brilliance was a nonstarter.

Albrecht looked at me with the patient expression that one would use on a child who was being naive and pesky. "No, we don't have barrels. The people of Grǫfhamr are refugees, Jake; we came here with what we could carry on our backs and in a few Portable Holes that were snatched up on the run."

I raised my hands in surrender. "Ok, but we can just buy them from one of your shops, right? That whole broken economics thing works in Anundjå just like in Flobovia?"

Albrecht's patient look flickered into irritation but was almost instantly smoothed over into 'diplomat face' and a smile. "There are no shops in Grǫfhamr, Jake. None of the merchants we rescued were able to bring their shop with them, and without some way to acquire stock they couldn't restart them."

"Oh." I replied. I thought about that for a minute. "Well, we could teleport to Flobovia and buy things there, right? In fact, we could probably buy enough stock that you could start a store here, and then you'd have all the goods you need."

The face stayed carefully blank and the smile never flickered. "Money isn't a great priority when fleeing for one's life, Jake. Gold coins are heavy; most people grabbed food and weapons. And now that we've become part of Flobovia, I've returned all the plunder that we took in the initial invasion and I'm not about to raid for the supplies we desperately need. After all, that would be treasonous." The last was spoken in tones of such heavy irony that I very carefully chose not to acknowledge the topic at all.

Instead, I briefly outlined the ways in which the Flobovian (and, presumably, Anundjån) economic system were borked and could be ruthlessly exploited, thereby putting money in the "it ain't even a thing" category.

As I talked, Albrecht's eyebrows climbed higher and higher until I thought they were going to need an oxygen tank. "My god, I think that would work," he murmured in amazement. He paused for a long moment and got a faroff look. "We could have enough weapons, enough armor, and decent food. No more created protein glop. Everyone could eat well, for every meal. My god." His voice was filled with pure wonder.

I smiled; I might not do strategy terribly well, but I could make the rules of this world roll over and beg for mercy. From what I had gleaned, the people who lived here had grown up with those rules and, accepting them as normal, had never searched for exploits. Having an outside view gave me an enormous advantage.

That thought sparked another. My outside view was, in large part, derived from my otherworldly knowledge of D&D as a game and the rules thereof. (Well, that and a whole lot of ideas and exploits bandied about on the Internet, but that was neither here nor there.) If I was weak on strategy, then I needed a book on strategy from Earth. The obvious choice was "The Art of War"; I had, in point of fact, read it, but it had been a long time ago. I reached back into my memory for the text.

I came up empty. I could remember fragments of it, but I couldn't bring to mind the full details. I frowned; my memory had been flawless ever since the intelligence boosts, what was the problem?

A moment's thought gave me the likely answer: the memories I had called to mind before now were either very recent (the taste of the cheese I had after arriving here) or very personally important (memories of childhood and family). "The Art of War" was neither of those things. Apparently, I didn't suddenly have an eidetic memory, it was just that my brain was more richly connected and therefore had more routes to reach things that were relatively easily accessible.

Maybe they are normal for a guy my age, but performance problems are so embarrassing.

"Ok, assume that that solves the supply problem," I said, shelving the idea for future consideration and and returning to proximate issues. "Let's get back to the undead; I can think of lots of ways to kill them with minimal risk to us. What's your strategy and how do I best fit into it tactically?"

"The ideal action would be a decapitation strike," Albrecht stated without hesitation. "Eliminate the Lich Kings—especially Afi—and leave the enemy leaderless. If they lacked overall command we could separate them and defeat them in detail. It would take a long time, but I'm confident it could be done; the wights are easy to kill, it's the incorporeals—wraiths, shadows, ghosts, what have you—that are a problem, but even there we could do it if we were careful."

He paused for a moment, lost in his thoughts. "Killing the liches is pretty unlikely though. Next best would be to take out some of their strongest casters. My grandnephew, Colborn Löfgren—he'd be an excellent target. A Fourth Circle Master High Mage, focus in battlemagic; now he's a vampire and he spends most of his time hunting us. No strategic sense, but an expert tactician with a good sense of who and what to target. He served under me in the Dwarf War; I would assign an undercommander to get him to the battle and then turn him loose. He was also an expert at tracking insurgents, so he's a major problem for us. Five of our last sixteen times moving Grǫfhamr, it's because he tracked us down."

He started pacing, hands clasped behind his back in thought. The room wasn't really big enough for pacing, so it was more a question of step-step-turn than real pacing. "Then, of course, there's Astrid. I mentioned her earlier; expert biomage. Some of the creatures she's developed as infiltrators and spies have been a real problem. There are others; High Mage Calder, expert diviner. Hertha Knutsen; High Cleric, focus on weather magic. Kelby Hjort; generalist mage and explorer. He knows far too many of the good hiding places in this country."

He stopped pacing and turned to face us; the hands came out from behind his back in a shrug. "The list goes on. Almost all of our top magi and clerics; literally millions of casters more powerful than anything Flobovia has ever seen. They represent too much of a strategic and tactical advantage for the drauga; we can't afford to engage the enemy in battle unless we can be sure of wiping the targets out in the initial attack, before a Sending can be sent out. Otherwise, a party of High Magi and High Priests will teleport in and we're all dead. It's a problem; I'd like to be level grinding our people with small engagements, but we simply can't take the risk."

I shrugged. "Ok, well, killing these guys off ain't really a thing. We get within a few miles of them and use an antimatter strike to blow them all off the map from way outside any range they could sense us at. Let's do the Liches first; you said they were the most important. Do we know where any of them are?"

Thomas shook his head. "Not as easy as that, I'm afraid. Liches have phylacteries; usually more than one. When a lich is destroyed, it reforms from its phylactery. And the phylacteries are always ridiculously well hidden." He paused, then clearly decided to finish his thought. "Also, I don't think blowing up all our highest level magi is a good idea."

I grunted in disgust. "First off, I know how to do it without the mage dying. Second, that phylactery thing is ginormously annoying, and someone who isn't me should figure out how to stop it from happening. But, ok, leave that aside for a minute. Albrecht, will destroying craptons of junior undead help? Maybe we wipe out a bunch of minions, wait for some of your big names to teleport in, then wipe them out with an antimatter blast? We could probably honey trap a lot of them that way, with minimal risk."

He nodded and grimaced simultaneously. "It might work, but the incorporeals will be immune to your 'antimatter explosion' attacks; no non-magical attack can hurt them. Because of that, there's a good chance that any of the casters teleporting in would arrive in an incorporeal form, just as insurance."

I chuckled. "That's ok, I've got plenty of ideas for magical attacks. So, tell me O Mighty Master of Military Mayhem: assuming that I can blow the crap out of the bad guys—because, you know, I totally can—where and when do I get to do it?"

Albrecht eyed me for a moment. "The first thing we need to do is get a shop set up in Grǫfhamr so we can get the physical things—especially food—that we need. The morale and spirit of the army is the single greatest factor in war; without it, no army can succeed. The conditions in Grǫfhamr, and of the war in general, have seriously sapped the troops' spirits, which is the other half of why I haven't been willing to take them out to fight."

Thomas and I both raised an eyebrow at that. "You seemed to be doing fine when you butchered Oxport and Tor Canle," Thomas contended in a voice made of glaciers and knives.

Albrecht shrugged, his mind clearly elsewhere. "That was different. The troops knew that they weren't going to be fighting overwhelmingly powerful drauga casters and they were going to be fighting for a place to live that was far more comfortable than here—a place for themselves and their families both. I had my pick of volunteers for that. If I had to lead a force against the drauga there are maybe a thousand that I would be willing to trust, out of well over a million troops under arms."

Thomas was starting to seethe at the casual dismissal of Tor Canle and Oxport, so I stepped in hastily before this could devolve into another poky-object-in-place-where-poky-object-should-not-be conversation. "Ok, so getting a shop set up is first priority. How many of the Flobovian magi that we brought with us made it here? Most of them are higher level, so their Rope Tricks will last long enough to help relieve some of the crowding."

"About four hundred," Albrecht responded, gratefully turning away from Thomas's thunderous expression. "We've put them on making living space and cleanup duty. They aren't happy about it, but they haven't actively started trouble yet."

"Ok, good," I responded, satisfied that things were mostly working out. "So, we get the store set up; that means everyone can eat decent food again, which should help a lot. After that, we kill off the Lich Kings—even if it's only at least temporary it buys us some time while the enemy is leaderless. During that time, we wipe out tons and tons of drauga, just to whittle their numbers down. That last part should be easy enough—we find a big group of them, set off an antimatter explosion that vaporizes every undead within miles, then we move on to the next group and do it again. Sounds like a plan." I nodded decisively, pleased at this handy summation and the clearcut action plan it entailed. "Yep, easy peasy."

I should have known better. Calling anything about this situation "easy peasy" was a bit like blowing a raspberry at Murphy while mooning him and saying that his father smelt of elderberries.

"Not a good idea," Albrecht insisted. "Those antimatter explosions of yours are too powerful—they chew up large portions of the landscape. Too much of that and one of the gods of land, river, forest, or so on will show up to object."

I groaned. "Damnit, you and your freakin' gods. Fine!" I started pondering the feasibility of airbursts, but quickly rejected the idea. For one thing, it might possibly piss off some god of sky, or rainclouds, or mosquitoes, or let's-make-Jake-miserable. (Because there was clearly a god specially devoted to that!) For another, it would be too tricky to calculate the height where we would catch a good number of undead in the explosion while not damaging the landscape too much. I was pretty sure that radiation wasn't going to affect the undead, so only the fireball and shockwave would matter. That meant we needed to get the fireball up close and personal.

"Hang on," I said, with a sudden surge of hope. "I've already done the antimatter thing twice, and no one showed up to smack me. Doesn't that mean it's safe?"

Albrecht shook his head. "No. You blew up Herrún's temple; she's not around to object, but her temple was still her temple, meaning it was outside the purview of any other god. As to the mountain...." He shrugged. "I don't know. Maybe the earth goddess and the mountain god were fighting about who got to respond and never got around to actually responding. Maybe the mountain god decided to treat it as a mining effort. Or maybe he just slept through it—it can be hard to get his attention, sometimes. But I certainly wouldn't risk it again without better information."

"Fine," I grumbled. "We won't blow them up." My voice dropped to a disgruntled mumble as I talked mostly to myself. "Stupid gods and their stupid rules about stupid things like not blowing up stupid undead." Sighing, I went back to the conversation around me. "Ok, well, I can think of plenty of other ways to kill tons of them that won't involve so much collateral damage."

Albrecht cocked his head to the side, thinking about what I was saying. Suddenly his eyes got wide and sparkled with manic glee as his face split open in a smile of clever delight.

"I've got a better idea," he began with excitement. "Thomas, tell me a little more about the Landguard and their swords...."

The falling vampire blocked the light that entered through the overhead entrance of the Portable Hole; it cast weird shadows on the Landguard waiting below as it thrashed and struggled to throw off Guardian Second Jaqueline Marsh.

They hit the ground together; Jacqueline gave a grunt as they impacted, but did not loosen her grip.

The vampire had been taken by surprise, but it was no fool; it realized almost immediately that it could not escape the woman's enhanced strength. Instantly, it began to dissolve into a greasy black mist. Even before the creature was fully transformed, the mist was streaming for the surface in a frantic bid for escape.

Paladins they might be, but the Landguard had not the slightly interest in playing fair. Before the transformation could complete, a strident bong rang out and all magic ceased, jolting the creature back to its material form. Simultaneously, the entrance of the Hole vanished, the magic portal shut off by the wave of antimagic. Mentally off balance and confused by the sudden cessation of its powers, the vampire was too slow to react as it was forced back into its corporeal form. The three Landguard descended on it like wolves on a sick cow, delivering powerful strikes with fists, feet, and lead-weighted saps.

The vampire fought back, but it was confused and weakened by the antimagic; within moments, it was unconscious on the bottom of the Hole.

Duncan studied it with a practiced eye. "Kick it a few more times," he growled. "Let's be sure it's not faking." All three Landguard gathered around and put the boot in repeatedly until the vampire was bruised, battered, and most definitely not faking its unconsciousness.

Once he was sure the undead leech was secured, Duncan nodded to the third member of his team; Guardian First Philip Archer gave a crisp acknowledgement and tapped his swords together, deactivating their antimagic. The opening to the Hole popped back into existence above them, but only for the instant that it took Jacqueline to reach up and pull it in after them, sealing them in the extradimensional space. Against the floor to one side, a small brass pipe resumed sending out the gentle but steady stream of fresh air which kept the Hole from becoming a suffocating death trap.

Duncan reached into his Handy Haversack and tossed a ridiculously overdone helm to Philip. Made of brightly polished silver and shaped like a giant sea shell, the thing was covered with intricately-tooled gold inlay, encrusted with a ridiculous number of horribly gaudy gems (most of them actually semi-precious stones, and not high quality ones at that), and crowned with a three-foot spray of peacock feathers. It was the tackiest magic item any of them had ever seen, almost eye-searing in its ugliness. The mage who had crafted it must never have put any ranks in Knowledge Skill (Good Taste)...and might have been blind drunk as well.

The vampire had been a tall, dark-skinned man with deepset eyes and a weak chin. Not terribly handsome to begin with, he looked like a drunk clown once Jacqueline jammed the helm down on his head.

"Detect Evil," muttered Duncan, eying the unconscious monster critically. After a moment he shook his head. "Didn't take; he must have made his Will save. Try it again. And give him a whack while you're at it; he's healing up."

Jacqueline frowned in irritation before yanking the helm off the vampire's head and immediately jamming it back on. Ring! went the helm as Philip booted the monster in the head to keep it unconscious.

Duncan shook his head. "Nope. Still didn't work; try it again."

The helm got yanked off and slapped on again. Ring! went the helm as another kick landed.

"Nope." Ring!

"Nope." Ring!

"Nope." Ring!

"Nope." Ring!

"Nope." Ring!

"Nope." Ring!

"Got it! Ok, let him wake up."

Moments later, the vampire's eyes blinked open in confusion. It took a moment for them to uncross; Philip had kicked him pretty hard, a lot. Once he was seeing clearly again, the monster studied the three warriors standing over it, looking tense and holding their blades in hand with the easy grip of accomplished masters. The monster very carefully did not move while it assessed its situation.

"Um...hi?" it ventured.

"Welcome back, Master High Mage Löfgren," Duncan offered in his trademark growl. "Your granduncle sends his best."

Colborn sat up, rubbing his head gently. Looking around, he saw the helm lying next to him and nodded in sudden understanding.

"Helm of Opposite Alignment, right?" he wondered. "That's why I'm suddenly Lawful Good? Thanks, by the way."

Duncan grunted an acknowledgement. The two junior Landguard relaxed and sheathed their swords.

"Come on," Duncan said, offering a hand to pull the still-vampiric-but-no-longer-evil Master High Mage Löfgren to his feet. "Let's get you back to headquarters. This should be fun."
