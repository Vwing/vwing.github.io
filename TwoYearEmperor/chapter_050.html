My armchair was ridiculously comfortable—heavily overstuffed, soft velvety cover, armrests at just the right height. I lounged, my feet up on an equally perfect ottoman, sipping my chai latte and enjoying Allison's toasty heat. Occasionally I would ponder getting up, sauntering over to the dark cherry shelves a few yards away, and taking down one of the thousands of leather-bound books that waited for me, pages full of exciting stories and absorbing science, art, math....

I'm not sure how long it took before I noticed the wrongnesses about the room. For one thing, where in Stupidworld would I get a chai latte? Second, the last thing I remembered, I was still in Grǫfhamr—where would I find a room yards across? And why would Allison be here? And why was everything fuzzy and distorted?

"You're dreaming, dumbass," Allison said.

I pondered that for a couple of minutes, trying to understand it.

"Huh?" I asked.

Allison gave a great, heaving sigh. "Dreaming, dork. D-R-E-A-M-I-N-G. Do me a favor? Don't piss her off. I really don't want to be rended into tiny little aetheric fragments and scattered across every layer of the Abyss."

I frowned. That sounded like a bad thing.

"Not exactly dreaming, little salamander," the woman in the chair across from mine put in quietly. "More like the potential for a dream. I'm only a potentiality myself for now, so I can't very well create a real dream." She was hard to understand; her voice blurred, as though there were other people saying the same or similar words at the same time.

I looked over to her slowly; I was feeling disconnected and it was hard to focus on anything.

I didn't know who she was, but I had a feeling that I should know, and that it was important that I figure it out. Whoever she was, she was striking—six foot one, silver hair, and a face that was unlined and somewhat horsey—long and square, with a masculine chin. She was looking at me, expectantly.

"Say hello, Jake! For the love of the Flame, say hello!" Allison urged, throwing a spark onto my hand as a prompt. I jumped, shook my hand, and stuck it in my mouth to soothe the pain.

Obnoxious or not, Allison's little ouchie had helped cut through some of my logginess. "You're...Herrún," I guessed, still having to push through a mental fog to articulate that.

Allison hurried into the conversational breach. "Please excuse him, Your Ladyship, he's a bit of an idiot. What he meant to say, Your Ladyship, was 'Welcome, Your Ladyship, it's an honor to meet you.' Right, Jake?" Her voice was quite pointed, and a little bit scared.

Herrún smiled at her. "Relax, little salamander. I have no quarrel with you. I've always enjoyed your kind...your True Names are such delicious secrets, and you learn so much during your various sojourns on the Prime Material. Over the centuries you accumulate great knowledge and many secrets. All this, yet such a straightforward people; I find it most refreshing."

"Ah, well," said Allison, sounding much relieved. "That's good. Um...as to Jake, could you go easy on him? He's a dork, but I kinda like him. Please?"

She waved Allison quiet and turned to face me. "Yes, Jacob. I am Herrún, Lady of Secrets. And we need to talk."

I was pretty sure that those words were bad, but I couldn't remember why. My head was fuzzy and scattered, as though someone had spun the focus knob all the way off.

"Why'm I so out of it?" I asked, having to concentrate hard to keep my thoughts organized long enough to turn them into sounds.

She smiled...sadly? Ironically? I couldn't focus enough to tell. "Like I said, potential for a dream. It's not quite happening, and yet it's happening to all of the 'yous' that could exist right now, if you had made slightly different choices. The various versions of you are overlapping a bit, hence the confusion."

I tried to follow that, but couldn't; my thoughts skittered away as I tried to grasp the concepts.

I abandoned the effort and instead groped around for what I should say next. There was something I should be asking, but what...?

Oh, right. "Why are you here?" I asked.

She nodded in slight approval. "We need to talk about you and your decisions," she told me, steel in her voice. "You blew up my temple, Jake. The greatest repository of knowledge and secrets in the world, and my last foothold of existence on this plane. In the process, you killed eleven hundred and thirty one people, all of them full of secrets. Among which were five pregnancies that no one knew about yet—rather large secrets, considering the impact they would have had on those people's lives." There was something in her eyes that promised doom; it wasn't as terrifying as what I had seen when Loki had decided to make his presence felt, but baby tigers grow into adult tigers given time.

I tried, mostly unsuccessfully, to muse on that for a minute. "Um," I said brilliantly.

"In fairness," she continued, "you were laboring under a mind rewrite at the time. As a result, I'm going to give you a stay of execution. But you'd better make it up to me, Jake. In a big way." The threat was far more obvious now, the claws flexing visibly.

I focused hard, bringing to bear every scrap of my amplified intelligence to push through the haze. "I already decided what I would sacrifice to you. Is that enough?"

She frowned; her eyes glowed blue-white and her fingers twitched as though sorting through threads. She faded as she did, becoming translucent. As she did, the fuzziness in my head receded and the room around me vanished into a cloud of fog.

"It's too far out for me to know it yet. Say it," she demanded.

I nodded, finally feeling clear enough to lay out something more than six words long. It still required focus, but I could put the thoughts together.

"Simple: take off and nuke the site from orbit. It's the only way to be sure." I was pretty proud of myself for the allusion.

She raised an eyebrow. "What?"

I leaned forward a bit, blinking and forcing my thoughts into alignment again. I was better than I had been, but still muzzy-brained.

"Find a new world for us and move all of Grǫfhamr's people there," I began. "Then use Polymorph Any Object to create about a couple trillions tons of antimatter to vaporize this entire planet—funny thing about PAO, it has a size limit on the source object but not on the destination object so, yeah." I shook my head in disgust at the yet-more-examples of stupidity in Stupidworld's magic. "It would kill the liches and destroy their phylacteries so they couldn't come back...well, assuming their phylacteries are somewhere here instead of on another plane. The incorporeals would survive, of course, but they'd be left floating in empty space in full sunlight."

Allison choked. Herrún turned to her, one eyebrow raised and an expression that was not-quite-yet annoyed. Allison's flame contracted in on itself, huddling into a tiny flicker; Herrún studied her for a moment longer, then nodded in satisfaction and turned back to me, considering my words.

She tipped her head in thought; vaguely, I noticed that she had grown more solid as I spoke, and I could now make out details of the way her hair shifted when she moved her head. "I see. And how would you locate the new world for your people?"

I shrugged. "The Stupi...this world's universe is, presumably, as large as my own—nigh infinite, for practical purposes. There're also other realities parallel to this one, mine being one of them, but they're all variations on the Prime Material Planes and most likely not other planes per se. Assuming this is something like the Copenhagen Model, the number of these realities is probably either infinite or so large that it might as well be infinite."

I paused to make sure she was following. She nodded impatiently, making a 'get on with it' gesture with one hand. I hurried to continue. It might be best to let sleeping dogs lie, but it was even best-er to keep impatient goddesses happy, 'only a potentiality' or not.

Especially since I was the one who'd blown up her temple and I had a feeling that she was going to kill me if I wasn't really super awesome nice to her.

"I'd talk to an artist," I told her. "Describe a made-up imaginary world for him, have him draw it. Somewhere in the multiverse, that world almost certainly exists. With the picture, you can use Greater Scrying to locate that world. Use Greater Teleport to send a mage there—with proper protections against the environment, of course—so she can scout it and make sure it's safe. Once we know that it is, use Grǫfhamr to transport the surviving Deorsi there.

"Once everyone was safely there, send one mage back here to blow the place up," I finished. Immediately, I got worried that she'd be offended by the idea of me using a suicide bomber, so I hurred to correct that impression. "She could Shapechange into a ghost or some other incorporeal so as to be immune to the blast, and then Greater Teleport to us once the world was gone."

She nodded, intrigued. "What about the gods of the undead? Wouldn't they strike you down after you did this?"

I shifted uncomfortably in my dream-chair. "Well...maybe. But there seems to be a link between the power of the god and the number of worshippers. It might just be that more powerful gods attract more worshippers, but what if the link is causal and goes in the other direction? What if number of worshippers dictates the power of the god? In that case, you would have destroyed all of the evil gods' worshippers and they'd be dead or powerless. Either way, no threat. And...it seems plausible. You...died?...vanished?...became whatever it is you are now, when all of your worshippers vanished. Again, could have been that they vanished because you went away, but I thought maybe it went the other way."

She looked me right in the eyes while nodding slowly; her lips curled into a small smile as she did. "Interesting thought; I can't confirm it, of course. But you said you'd save the Grǫfhamrites; what about the people of Arannias—the Flobovians, Tarisians, and so on? Why not rescue them as well?"

I grimaced but forced myself to say it. The words came out slowly, my rationality forcing them past my traditional moral sense; from a utilitarian viewpoint, this was correct; from a normal-society-morality viewpoint it was horrific. "I thought about that, but it would be better not to. They worship too many different gods, and some of those people are evil and worship evil gods—possibly even the ones we're facing now. Those gods might have an incentive to smack us; there's no way to be sure. If we keep the survivors confined to the gods of Grǫfhamr, we can be sure that all the surviving gods are on our side. And, under the conditions that prevail in Grǫfhamr, it's vanishingly unlikely that any of the people here are evil, or worship any of the enemy gods. Everyone is too tightly packed; the evil ones would have been found out and executed."

"Hmmm," she said, steepling her fingers. She was looking more solid by the minute, and the room around me was looking...present, although still shrouded in thick fog. "What if the evil gods had worshippers on other planes, enough worshippers to keep them strong after you destroyed their worshippers here?"

I shrugged. "We're not going to win this war without taking some risks. According to Albrecht, the gods of the undead are 'nigh-omnipotent'. Yet they haven't taken any direct action against us yet; why not? There must be something that prevents them from acting, or we'd all be dead already. Whatever that is, why wouldn't it continue after those gods were weakened?"

One divine eyebrow went up and the fingers tapped against each other in thought. "Interesting. Did Master Sage Einarr tell you all this about gods?"

I shook my head. "No, this is all my thinking. I haven't shared it with anyone yet."

She nodded thoughtfully. "Interesting indeed. Very well. Why did you decide to sacrifice this secret?"

"It's too horrible," I admitted. "Rationally, it's the right thing to do. The odds are too high against us; if we're right about the Polymorph engines creating limitless undead, then we can't win by attrition. The Lich Kings are dangerous enough on their own, but we can't actually kill them unless we can figure out where their phylacteries are, and we don't have any reasonable way to do that." I paused. "Unless you felt like telling us?" I asked hopefully.

She shook her head with a sardonic smile. "My domain is secrets and knowledge. Right now I'm only a potentiality; I can't afford to weaken myself any further by just giving away any of my domain."

I shrugged. "Didn't think so. Anyway. If we can't find their phylacteries we can't take out the Lich Kings. Without being able to take them out we can't shut off their spell engines—they would just rebuild. Given time, they'll kill every living thing on the planet; destroying the planet would at least allow us to save something. The utility calculations say that I should do it. I just...can't. I'm just not cold blooded enough. I can't kill practically every human, all the dogs and cats, all the baby seals, destroy all the art and libraries that all the sentient life has accumulated. It's too much. But, if things got bad enough...I would. I don't want to die, and I'm afraid that eventually it would start to look appealing."

"Hm," she murmured, pursing her lips in thought. I waited for what she would say next, trying not to wring my hands in nervousness.

Finally, she seemed to come to a conclusion. "All right," she stated. "The only way you know to save the lives of yourself and millions of others, while defeating a massive evil that threatens to destroy the world? An important secret indeed. Very well, sacrifice that secret to me and I'll forgive your destruction of my temple." She met my eyes for a moment and my guts turned to melted Jello. "Do not act counter to My interest in the future, Jacob. I will not forgive you again."

I nodded frantically, over and over like a demented bobblehead doll until she faded from view. Those terrifying, soul-devouring eyes were the last things to fade away. Once they finally did, I found myself gasping in relief and....

...I jerked upright in bed, slamming my head on the too-close ceiling and collapsing back into the pillow. The bedding was soaked in sweat, and the light sheet—all that was necessary in the constant, oppressive heat of Grǫfhamr—was tangled around me like swaddling. Thomas and Duncan were standing next to me looking concerned; over their shoulders I could see half a dozen other Landguard packing the doorway and hall.

I wasn't really paying attention to that, though, being far more concerned about the blood pouring from my nose and the blinding pain of what I was pretty sure was a concussion. Possibly a split-open skull. Maybe I was dying? That sounded pretty good right now.

"Ow," I muttered, the word muffled by the blood that was stuffing up my nose.

Thomas's lips twitched in amusement and Duncan snorted. The crusty old Sergeant—a.k.a., 'that sadistic bastard'—poked me in the nose with one finger, inducing a white-hot flare of agony, and muttered "Lay On Hands." The familiar sensation of warm golden light washed through me, the pain vanished, and my nose was whole again.

"You couldn't have poked me in the arm?" I muttered grumpily.

Duncan laughed. "You're welcome."

"Gotta be a dungheap around here somewhere," I grumbled, swinging my legs off the bed and dropping carefully to the floor. (For the record; standing in between two armored-up hyper-fit superwarriors in your boxers and deskjockey belly is an uncomfortable and embarrassing experience. I recommend against it.)

"We need to talk to Albrecht," I told them, pulling my jeans on and revelling in their cleanliness and suppleness. Having seen that Prestidigitation trick the Grǫfhamrites used to clean the walls I had asked Bob if he knew the spell. He did, and was happy to use it to thoroughly launder my otherworldly clothes. It meant that I could wear them every day from now on and not have to worry about the stupid fasteners of Stupidworld's stupid clothing.

I shrugged into my trenchcoat and strode out of the room, Thomas and Duncan falling into place in front and behind.

"So that's the story," I finished. "I'm pretty sure that it wasn't just a dream. Too linear and detailed for that, and the whole 'divine death stare of deadly dire doom' thing was too convincing to be just a memory."

"It does sound like a true vision," Master Sage Einarr concurred.

"Ok, what do we make of it? Also, how are we coming on the ritual? And that whole 'not being detected and killed' thing? And—"

Albrecht held up a hand. "One at a time, Jake. To answer your question, we realized that we were being foolish about the fear of detection. The Sky Slayer solved this problem long ago; they fly, high enough that their Weirdstone doesn't affect the ground, and they are constantly moving. We don't happen to have an airship handy, but when the time comes we'll have one of our mages use Overland Flight to take Grǫfhamr into the sky. Every few hours we'll swap magi and keep the city in the air from then on."

I frowned. "How will you swap magi? If he's carrying it, the city is closed. For that matter, how will we breathe? I thought there was only a little bit of air in here without the door open."

"We took your suggestion about the Darsson's Cooling Breeze spells," Unn supplied, seeming pleased. "We set up thousands of them, all through the city, and used your Hourglass to make them permanent. We're now entirely independent of outside air."

Thomas frowned. "The city is huge. How did you get the Hourglass to all the...." He broke off, nodding in understanding. "You formed another railgun to move the Hourglass around, didn't you?"

Albrecht nodded. "Yes. We can't take off until the ritual is complete, though. It's in progress now; any disruption will cause it to fail."

I raised an eyebrow at that. "I was expecting it to take more time than that to get everything organized. Didn't you have to set up schedules for who would be moving when, so that there was enough room in the halls?"

Albrecht shrugged. "We're a group of refugees organized on military lines. We're good at making schedules."

"Huh. So, how does that work, exactly?" I asked, curious.

"With the reorganization of the city, the distance to the door is less than a standard move for most of the residents," Drifa told me. "People can run out the door, drop their secret in the fire on their assigned altar, say their prayer, and keep running into the segment of Grǫfhamr that was set up opposite their starting point. They run as deep as they can into the city, then climb up into one of the Rope Tricks so that they can stop without being in anyone's way. By having people running in from the left and out from the right, we can make it so that only the door is a choke point. It's wide enough to fit two people if they squeeze and we've already moved sixty or seventy thousand people through. At this rate, the ritual should be complete in about four days."

"I must say, it's a very efficient method; I congratulate you all," said the brown-haired half-elf who had just sauntered into the room. He leaned carelessly against the wall by the door, arms folded and smiling.

Before his back touched the wall, Alpha Squad was on him, blurring towards him with blades out.

They didn't even get close.

A thin black ribbon of force appeared in the air in front of the intruder, stretching from wall to wall; it came into existence so close to the charging paladins that they never had a chance to dodge. Robert and Rob, leading the charge, got clotheslined flat and went down in a heap, tangling the others and preventing them from closing the distance.

Thomas surged to his feet and drew his blades as he moved around the table to get between me and the stranger. Duncan's swords were already in his hands; before Thomas could get around me, Duncan tapped his blades together, shutting down all magic in the area. Instantly, the room vanished as we were dumped out of the Magnificent Mansion we'd been sitting in, fell into the enclosing segment of Grǫfhamr (Violet #4791, as it happened), and that Portable Hole was shut down and separated from the rest of the city.

The Hole contained me, Duncan, and the stranger. I was pressed against the back wall; had I been one seat to the right I would have been in the next segment and safe. As it was, I was going to have the opportunity to watch Sergeant Duncan administer a beatdown to someone who wasn't me. I felt a little sorry for the guy, really.

The stranger gave Duncan a smile and a Jedi wave. "Relax, my friend. I'm no threat. You don't really want to attack me, do you? It seems like such an unnecessary action. I'm sure we can find a peaceful way to coexist if you'll just refrain from fighting for a minute or so."

Duncan arrested his momentum, straightened up, and smiled. A friendly smile. I almost fainted.

The stranger smiled back; he pushed himself up from the wall and stretched out a hand to Duncan. "Won't you be kind enough to turn off that anti-magic field, friend? If you'll forgive me, it seems a little rude."

Duncan looked abashed. I almost fainted again.

"Right, of course. Sorry, sir," he said, tapping his swords together in embarrassment. No longer suppressed by his antimagic, the Mansion blinked back into visibility around us, along with the other inhabitants of the room. Every one of them looked alarmed.

"Hello all," the stranger said. "I'm here peacefully, with no intent to fight. Can't we be friends?"

Everyone relaxed back into their seats, smiling genially.

"Pleased to meet you," Thomas said. "Let me introduce you. I'm Thomas Rheinhart, Commander of the Landguard. This is High Marshal Albrecht Löfgren, his grandnephew Colborn, Master High Mage Unn Lager, Master High Priestess Drifa Hummel and, last but not least, His Imperial Majesty Jacob, Emperor of Flobovia. Who might you be, sir, and what can we do for you?"

The stranger waved in a friendly fashion. "Well, taking those in reverse order, I'm here to do a little diplomacy; I think your people and mine have gotten off on the wrong foot, and we should really work together more. As to who I am, my name's Afi."


Author's Note part 2: For the benefit of non-D&D players: there is a skill called Diplomacy which allows the user to change the attitude of NPCs as regards himself. If you're *really* good at it, you can take someone who is Hostile to you (meaning, 'willing to take risks in order to hurt you') and make them Helpful ('willing to take risks to help you') in the span of six seconds. It's utterly terrifying how completely broken it is.

Needless to say, Afi is *really* good at it.
