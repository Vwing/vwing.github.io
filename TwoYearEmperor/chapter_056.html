It brought the netting down a bit and made the camoflage pattern on the outside blend less well into the surroundings.

Thomas wasn't letting me anywhere near the outside, but I could hear Duncan's bellow even from the deepest reaches of the city. Of course, this was less impressive than it might have seemed, given the fact that after our reorganization the "deepest reaches of the city" were only a few dozen yards from the outside. This fact had Thomas sweating bullets right now; he had blockaded the corridor in front of me with over a hundred Landguard bodies, all with their weapons in hand. The remainder were all helping to set up the netting.

"You let Duncan off punishment duty, huh?" I asked, just to break the nervous silence.

Thomas shrugged without looking away from the door. "Say what you like about him, he's really good at kicking untrained people into shape and getting them to do what needs to be done."

I nodded fervent agreement.

In truth, it was a good thing that I could hear Duncan, since his bellowing was about the only thing that we could tell about what was going on. I'd tried to have a mage named Sigmund set up a JARVIS system for me—use Scry Location to see the outside of Grǫfhamr, then relay what he saw into a Major Image for the rest of us. For whatever reason, that hadn't worked; the magic simply failed to manifest. He'd also tried an Arcane Eye, but that had also failed. The failure had everyone on the edge of freaking out, most especially Thomas. When he demanded of Albrecht if this was normal, Albrecht had shrugged and said, "We don't typically scry close in to the city, for fear that it might be detected. We use physical scouts for in close." Thomas had nearly killed him on the spot in sheer frustration.

Given this lack of direct knowledge, everything I know about what happened is based on the after-action debrief that we had given Duncan and the other Landguard who were outside at the time it all went down. Working from their descriptions, Sigmund was using a Major Image to throw a composite view of the events up on the JARVIS screen to aid in understanding.

Sigmund set down the last transcript and made a few final adjustments to the Image. "Remember," he warned us. "This is a depiction, not an exact rendering. I'm filling in details that weren't covered by the reports, so there might be differences between what I'm showing and the actual events." He nodded to Duncan. "Sergeant, if you see anything wrong, please point it out." Duncan nodded crisply.

With his warning issued, Sigmund started the story playing before us. I leaned back to watch, wishing I had popcorn.

The edges of the ravine were towering walls of red sandstone, a hundred feet high, veined with inclusions and the occasional fossil. The sun was westering, so everything was cast in gloomy shadow. The sand and gravel that covered the canyon's floor were being churned into spurts of gritty dust by the flying feet of the people sacrificing. They were racing through the ritual like greyhounds chasing the rabbit. A hundred yards farther down the ravine, groups of soldiers—including the rather terrifying Sergeant Duncan (who seemed even foul-mouthed and shorter-tempered today than usual)—scurried like ants as they busily set up the netting.

"You with the reinforcement panels! Line up! We want them all going up at once so the netting stays even!" Sergeant Duncan's face was red and spittle was flying from his mouth in the terrifying display of rage common to all effective drill instructors. "Ready an action to deploy on my command! You WILL deploy to your left side as you face the ritual! You WILL deploy on the command and not before or after! Because, so help me, if any of you stupid monkeys deploys early or late, I WILL RIP YOUR WORM-EATEN PUSTULENT LUNGS OUT THROUGH YOUR SKULL AND—" There followed a great deal of highly threatening profanity (see prior comment about the shortness of temper and the foulness of mouth) which left the men and women holding the rolled-up black cloth feeling very motivated indeed.

Time trickled by much more slowly than any of us would have preferred, leaving Duncan to glare at his well-motivated minions. Grǫfhamrites were still running back and forth across the narrow ravine, sacrificing to a goddess that did not currently exist. The black flames continued to crackle, their colors shifting briefly as each person made his or her sacrifice. The sun inched downwards, crawling across the sky with glacial speed.

Finally, after about a billion subjective years, a sparrow made of smoke appeared in front of Duncan and whispered, "Ready." Stepping to the side of the ravine Duncan sucked in a lungful of air and, in his best drill-sergeant command voice, he ordered the men and women at the netting to "DEPLOY! NOW NOW NOW, DEPLOY NOW!"

Those men and women shook out the rolled-up Portable Holes that they had been holding and slapped them up on the leaning-over netting. "UNSHRINK!" they all screamed.

It is worth doing a thought experiment about the mathematics of volume. A Portable Hole is six feet in diameter and ten feet deep, containing roughly two hundred and seventy cubic feet of space. A cubic foot can hold roughly eight gallons of water.

Now, imagine filling a Portable Hole with water—over two thousand gallons of it—and then touching that water with the Rule of Size and saying the remarkably silly word 'Reducio.' Instantly, the size of the water is decreased by a factor of sixteen in each dimension, leaving you with roughly two quarts of water; this is barely a puddle at the bottom of the Hole. Reduced water will not Reduce again, but you can still refill the Hole and Reduce the regular water. Do this enough times and you will now have two hundred and seventy cubic feet of space filled with water that is compressed to less than one four-thousandth of its normal size.

Now make that holy water, and restore its size all at once.

From each of the six Holes erupted a tsunami—nearly nine million gallons each, blasting forth all in an instant. The water howitzer arced up and over the altars and back down on the other side. Three of the men deploying the Holes, and one of the women, were not careful enough to be completely out of the way when they triggered the enlargement. They were effectively vaporized in the blast—whichever part of their body was in front of the water was torn cleanly off, sending them spinning into the water blast emerging from the Hole behind them; this instantly smashed their bodies into pulp and spread it over several hundred feet of downrange canyon.

The price to the enemy was far higher. Unseen by anyone present, Afi's Ice Assassins were floating invisibly back and forth above the altars, busily sacrificing every secret they possessed: the nature of their existence, the spells they had memorized, even the knowledge that they had spell slots. All of these secrets were being fed as quickly as possible—far more quickly than the Grǫfhamrites were managing—into the shell that would eventually become the Lady of Secrets. It was a brilliant plan that Afi had been maneuvering towards for a long time, and it was now blown thoroughly to hell.

As the water arced up and over the ritual area, the sky over the altars was filled with instantly-cut-off screams. Wisps of black vapor steamed into the sky, the last fleeting traces of the most powerful group of non-divine beings on the planet.

Cheers went up as the lich-clone-things were instantly erased from existence; it would have been better if we could actually have seen their bony bodies vaporize, but the screams and smoke trails were pretty satisfying all on their own.

Of course, a few of the Ice Assassins had been missed, through caution or good fortune on their part. A few of the ones towards the center happened to be low enough that the water passed over their heads. Some, more observant or just feeling more paranoid than the rest, had readied actions when they saw what was happening at the netting; they were able to cast various warding spells. As the water went by, spheres of pure magic, cubes of force, and immaterial draconic wings appeared, shielding their casters from the divinely empowered onslaught.

Unfortunately for them, Grǫfhamr wasn't done yet.

From atop the cliffs, a rain of large, warty amphibians—fenfrogs, to be exact—sluiced over the surviving liches. One of the first orders I'd given when arriving in Flobovia was to start harvesting the magic-eating critters from the Carr Fens; I had never rescinded that order, so Flobovians had been harvesting them for nearly three weeks now, passing them back up the commoner railgun to Capital City and stockpiling them. When we fought our way into Grǫfhamr, it had seemed like a good idea to bring some of them with us. It's quite surprising how many cat-sized frogs you can pack into thirty-one Portable Holes and a lot of Landguard-borne Handy Haversacks, Belts of Many Pouches, and various other extradimensional storage. (Although you do need to remember to put each 'frog in a sack first, so they keep their tongues to themselves and don't eat the magic of the item you're trying to put them in. That had been embarrassing.) Add a Darsson's Cooling Breeze to each of the Holes so that they don't suffocate, season with a Permanency from the Eternal Hourglass, and voilà! An ideal anti-lich weapon. I had actually intended it for use against Afi's vampire casters, but this was even better.

Sadly, it was a bit of a one-shot attack. The 'frogs were falling over a hundred feet; when they hit the ground they splatted like a bug on a windshield. Their last moments were happy ones, however, as they found themselves plummeting through the largest bounty of magic they had ever tasted. Amphibian tongues darted in all directions, flicking against protective spells that would have stopped a charging dragon and slurping them down like candy.

More importantly, the ones that came after them had unobstructed access to the liches themselves, and happily sucked the spellcasting ability right out of the undead archmagi. Over sixteen thousand frogs—eighty percent of our stockpile—plummeted to their doom; the liches never had a chance.

Appearances had had to be kept; setting up the nets and deploying the Portable Holes had required coming up with, and loudly announcing, plausible misdirections about camoflage netting. More importantly, it had not been possible to pause the flow of Grǫfhamrites through the ritual lest we raise the suspicions of the highly intelligent ice-liches. As a result, there were hundreds of people in the area under the frog strike. Most of them were clubbed unconscious by large amphibians landing on their heads and subsequently painfully battered by the following amphibious assault until the victims were buried under a solid layer of frogs. Fifteen people were killed instantly—having bad luck in the hit point lottery at birth is a great misfortune when thousands of cat-sized frog monsters come crashing down on you. It's also a really embarrassing thing to have to tell the other spirits in the afterlife: "So, Tom, what are you in for?" "Oh, I died in battle, holding the line at Eagle's Crag!" "Cool! How about you, Bill?" "Um...a frog fell on me. And I think my boss threw it."

"We need to know just how far ahead of us Afi is, so we know if we should punt or continue trying to out-sacrifice his icy little minions. A round of headshakes went around the table.

"We could get a specialist fire mage out there—perhaps someone with a fire affinity could tell more precisely, although I'm not sure. In any case, 'when is Afi going to show up' seems like the most important question in that list to me."

I nodded distractedly, trying to figure out how to measure time more accurately than available instruments allowed. Could we magic up an atomic clock? A superduper high speed camera? Time Stop would speed the user up until the world was frozen; could we use that to measure the change in the flames? What about Unn's idea of a specialist fire mage? Did Flobovia have one? Did I trust the Deorsi magi? Whoever we sent out might decide to lie and say that the enemy was too far ahead, just because he was scared and wanted to get relocated earlier. That couldn't be allowed; if there was any chance that we could make the ritual pay off, we needed to take it.

The answer hit me and I snickered. "Hang on, I think I know how we can measure the duration of the color change," I told them, standing up and waving for everyone to follow. "Bring a torch, we'll need it."

Thomas was next to me with an unlit torch in hand and a bemused expression on his face. The others were gathered around, also looking puzzled and staying out of the way as a swarm of Grǫfhamrites went racing back and forth around us. Just outside of that rush, the rest of the Landguard—by which I mean all of them—surrounded us in a series of concentric rings. I felt ridiculous.

Pushing that aside, I watched the flickers for a moment; nope, too fast for me to time them. I turned to Albrecht. "I need the teleport ward taken down for one round, can you do that?"

"That's unwise," he responded. "It's possible that Afi is probing us, constantly trying to teleport people in."

Ten minutes later we were back inside the war room—which was now inside a new Magnificent Mansion, inside a new node of Grǫfhamr, which had been thoroughly rearranged. Allison was sitting on a small dish of oil in the middle of the table and Unn was periodically using Prestidigitation to gather all the smoke into a little gray ball near the ceiling so that we didn't all suffocate. We'd gotten a lot of frowns and glares as we came in; apparently Allison was the only open flame in the entire city, smoke being a major issue in a sealed environment such as this. (It was clever how many workarounds they'd found: all light came from Continual Flame torches—which aren't really flame and produce no smoke—all cooking happened on top of steel slabs that had had Heat Metal cast on them, and so on.)

Allison had been read in—we'd had to; she kept asking snide questions until we explained what was going on—and we were back to our discussion.

"Ok, so, let's assume that we killed ninety percent of Afi's little MiniMe's with our Mega Water Dragon of Doom No Jutsu," I said, ignoring the strange looks I got at the name. If every single Grǫfhamrite goes through the ritual once, Afi will still have a one-third stake in what Herrún comes out like. That about right?"

Colborn shot her a glare but said nothing.

"Plus, he may have more Ice Assassins on the way here any time," Albrecht put in.

"Drifa," I asked, turning to her. "I know you said we couldn't, but just to check—there's absolutely no way that we can move the ritual, or start it up somewhere else while it's going on here, then stop this one?"

She shook her head decisively, the very image of a grizzled veteran politely refusing a stupid order from a green looie. "Regretfully Your Majesty, no. This ritual is pulling the fragments of the Lady to this spot; a second ritual elsewhere would just be fighting the pull. It would also be starting completely over in order to build up the same 'pull' that we've achieved so far.

Albrecht cocked his head in thought. "The ritual is pulling the Lady's fragments to this spot, or to the altars?" he asked.

Drifa paused. "Honestly, I'm not sure. Certainly to the altars, but I just don't know if the physical location is relevant."

"Give me your best guess," Albrecht demanded.

Drifa grimaced, but thought about it. "Well...probably the physical location isn't critical. It's the altars that define the locus; that said, moving the altars just isn't practical so it amounts to the same thing." She paused, then added. "We could have people sacrifice multiple secrets per pass, though. The number of secrets per person or per round isn't important, just that the flow continues."

Thomas faced me. "This would be a really good time for you to come up with one of your tricks, Jake," he told me frankly. "Without Lady Herrún on our side, we're going to lose this war eventually. I don't see how we can possibly hold off Afi for four days, and he's certain to notice the problem before then. Once he does, he'll show up and we're all dead."

I grimaced. "Yeah, thanks. No pressure." I stopped to think, and everyone waited.

I closed my eyes, cleared my mind, and focused on my breathing, forcing myself to relax. ~Fear is the mindkiller,~ I thought to myself with an inward twist of amusement. "~Fear is the little death that only cowards suffer. I will permit it to pass over me and through me, and then I will kick it righteously in the ass.~" The mangled Bene Gesserit mantra didn't help, but the humor did; I could feel myself relaxing.

Ok, if we had people start sacrificing multiple secrets per pass we could outbid Afi...assuming we could finish the ritual and have everyone do their sacrifices. Problem: we didn't have time for that. We needed some way to get people out from under the hammer and still finish the ritual.

I visualized the ritual and its surroundings; the flames, the people racing past it. Six altars, twelve Grǫfhamr nodes on the walls, a steady stream of people running in both directions. We could move the nodes easily enough; could we get all the people who had already sacrificed into one set of them, so that we could save at least some of the people? That didn't seem terribly practical; we were moving people between the nodes as fast as possible and packing them in.

Hm. Actually, we could fit all of them in one node, if we stacked the expansion spells. Put Rope Tricks inside Rope Tricks and you could fit an arbitrary number of people. Problem was, that was crazy dangerous. If Rope Trick Alpha contained Rope Trick Beta and Alpha expired while there were still people in Beta, what would happen? My guess was that the people in Beta would be permanently stranded. Even if it were safe, we'd have to significantly alter the flow of people in order for everyone with sacrifices made to all end up in a single set of nodes, and that might cause enough of a slowdown to disrupt the ritual. And we couldn't relocate all the nodes without giving up on the ritual.

I rolled my mental viewpoint down through the memory of the ravine, examining the walls, the floor, and the altars themselves. Could we pick up the altars and relocate them into Grǫfhamr? No, then they would be on a different plane and supposedly Herrún couldn't 'hear' them. She had managed to reach me while I was in Grǫfhamr, but that was in a 'potential dream', and I wasn't going to bet against Drifa's professional opinion.

Hm. 'Pick them up.' Something about that idea seemed promising.

My eyes popped open and I looked at Drifa. "Just how fragile is the ritual? If the altars were physically lifted, would that ruin it? Or if we carried them?"

She shook her head. "The ritual depends on each of the altars reinforcing each other, and its very sensitive. It would lose cohesion if the heights of the altars were more than a few inches different, or if any altar were rotated more than five or ten degrees out of alignment on any axis."

"So, as long as everything were kept completely aligned, they could be moved?" Colborn asked sharply.

Drifa hesitated; when she spoke the words were slow and unwilling. "In theory, yes, I suppose. But I doubt it could be done in practice," she said. "Even if you were able to keep everything correct, the flow of sacrifices couldn't stop, or even slow down appreciably. Frankly, I'm amazed that it survived the interruptions when you and Lord Loki made your sacrifices, Jake. I can only guess that those were powerful enough secrets to sustain the ritual across the time gap."

"Also, the altars are very heavy," Albrecht put in. "Several hundred pounds each, maybe a thousand. We'd need four bearers of exceptional strength, and we don't have anyone trained for that; I wouldn't think untrained bearers could keep it level, at the same relative height and not turned, while marching. If they fell out of step, the altar would pivot or twist. None of our people could stay that perfectly in step for long enough to matter."

"The Landguard could," Thomas put in. "When we're stationed in Capital City, a lot of our duties are ceremonial; we do a lot of close-order drill for parades and holidays. Also, one version of punishment duty consists of carrying the squad leader while he stands on a shield in full armor; if he wobbles or falls, the Guardsmen in question get used for targets in the hand-to-hand and archery practice. And we're strong enough that one Landguard could lift the altar, although two or four would be better for balance purposes."

I glanced at Thomas in surprise, one eyebrow shooting up. "'Get used for targets'? What does that mea...you know what, never mind. How fast could you walk, while keeping everything stable?" I asked.

"Certainly not faster than the Dirge March," he told me. "One step per second, about five feet per round."

"Fifty feet a minute, about half a mile per hour," I calculated thoughtfully. "That's not much, but it's something."

"If you're as skilled at formation marching as you say, then that would take care of the horizontal separation," Unn asked dubiously. "How would you handle sloped ground—it would cause vertical separation. Also, what about leveling? Here in Anundjå we use bubble levels, and they won't react fast enough to give the necessary precision."

"We could use a mercury level," I began, then paused. Where were we going to get mercury in a medieval tech level?

"Not a problem for a competent mage," Colborn interjected with a derisive sniff. "Just use Floating Disk; it will take care of keeping level, they will follow their casters at full movement speed, and they'll take care of staying level. We just need to worry about keeping them at the same height and in alignment with each other, and that's manageable—the height of the Disks can be adjusted to be anything up to three feet off the ground. As long as we don't have to go down too steep a slope there won't be an issue."

"We'll still need something to keep them level when they're being picked up to set them on the Disks," Thomas said. "Punishment drill doesn't require that degree of precision. What was that 'mercury level' you were talking about?"

I was smiling brightly by now; this was going to work! "Easy peasy. Put a drop of mercury in the center of a small plate," I told them. "Put the plate on the altar. As long as the mercury stays in the center of the plate, you're fine. And mercury is the material component for Floating Disk and it costs less than a gold piece, so the magi can produce as much as we need from their spell component pouches." ~And isn't THAT just annoying? Sure, here, have two million pounds of mercury! It's free!~ I thought to myself.

"Um, excuse me? May I ask a question?" Suze said, very timidly. At my nod she continued. "What about the people doing the sacrifices? We don't want everyone outside the city, and if they have to be jumping up onto the Disks they might bump the altars. It probably wouldn't matter, because the altars are so heavy, but it just seems, um, risky?"

Albrecht shrugged. "Good question, but it's manageable. We can do the same thing we did for the water cannons: we put the Portable Holes on vertical sheets and have a squad carry them. The people step from one side, onto the Disk, and into the other side." He paused, frowning. "No, we'll need to build a solid frame. If the substrate flaps in the breeze or, gods forbid, tips, the gravity will shift inside the city and someone could get hurt. That will be another thing that we'll have to be very careful to keep level."

The idea of gravity suddenly flipping ninety degrees throughout the city was not a pleasant one. I had a sudden vision of the frame with the Holes getting out of control, flipping over and over down a cliff, and everyone in the city being tumbled around like that one odd sock in the dryer. It was not a pretty picture and, based on everyone else's expression, I think they were all sharing it.

Eventually though, we got past it. Everyone looked around the table at each other, checking to see if anyone saw any other problems. When no one said anything I clapped my hands and said "I love it when a plan comes together! No time like the present. Albrecht, have someone find a new location for us. Thomas, if you and the Landguard can go get started on moving the altars...."

"Yeah, about that..." Loki said. "We need to talk."

"Indeed. And hopefully this conversation won't be as rude as the last," Afi chimed in.

Thomas and Alpha Squad blurred forward, blades in hand and I heard the bong! from Robert's direction as he shut down all magic.
