We all stared at her in disbelief.

"You're kidding, right?" I asked, utterly floored. I couldn't even begin to categorize the problems with her plan. "Do you have any idea how...that's just...argh!" I shook my head in emphatic disbelief...and nearly floored myself in a more literal fashion, as my queasy stomach did a flip and my hungover head stabbed me with an icepick.

"Fifty thousand," Albrecht put in. Even his sang froid seemed to be somewhat cracked. "Grǫfhamr is fifty thousand Portable Holes, nested together. That's a hundred linear miles of tunnels, and it doesn't even count all the extradimensional spaces inside that. And you can't teleport inside Grǫfhamr—teleportation spells can't cross planar boundaries, and each Hole is its own specific demiplane. There's just no way we could make that happen in any sort of practical timeframe."

"Well, first of all," she said with a smile, "do you have a better idea?"

There was a long silence.

"I didn't think so," she finished, clearly suppressing a triumphant grin. "Here's how I think we can make it work...."

And then she laid it out in detail, pulling out sheets and sheets of parchment with all the calculations sketched out in a hand so neat it almost looked typeset. We started off skeptical, progressed to dubious, and finally had to reluctantly admit that it probably was a workable, albeit Herculean, plan.

"The Landguard are the fastest people in Grǫfhamr," Thomas said. "Franklin, go give these plans to Sergeant Thorn. Get Third Company moving, on the bounce." Franklin nodded, scooped up the parchment, and turned for the door.

"Hang on," I called. Franklin checked himself and turned back to us. There was a more efficient way, but I wasn't about to make Thomas look bad in front of the Deorsi. Instead, I scribbled some orders on a spare sheet of parchment and added it to Franklin's stack. "Go," I told him. He vanished like smoke.

Drifa was human enough to preen just a little bit as the rest of us shook our heads in disbelief that we were actually going to execute on such a crazy plan.

Albrecht rapped the table with his knuckles to get our attention. "All right, back to work," he told us. "Next question: we need to figure out how to deal with the Lich Kings, especially Afi. Jake, any ideas?"

I winced at being put on the spot...and at the last stab of the headache that was finally starting to fade. "Aren't these Polymorph engines more important? If they have a limitless supply of undead, we need to shut off the flow ASAP. Shouldn't we be investigating that first?"

Albrecht shook his head. "Strategic, Jake, not tactical. You've demonstrated the ability to kill the minor drauga in job lots, and Colborn and I have some experience at that ourselves. We can deal with them later; the important thing is to take out the leadership, the ones who created those spell engines. Besides, if we destroyed the engines before the Lich Kings, they would just make more. We'd waste time and resources and be right back where we are now. So, any of your outside-this-world ideas on how to kill the Lich Kings?"

I hesitated. "Those filly-whoosamadingies that let them resurrect themselves—" I began.

"Phylacteries," Colborn supplied, rolling his vampiricly red eyes.

"Right, those," I replied, nodding appreciatively. "Unless we destroy those things, the liches just come back. We'd need to find them first, or there'd be no point in going after the liches at all. And I'm guessing that simple scrying won't do it, so we might have to...."

I stopped as a truly awful idea came to mind. I checked the Brainopedia to see if things were as I thought they were. Sadly, they were as I thought they were, which were actually not how I were hoping they were. I figured I'd get confirmation, in hopes that maybe this wasn't really a viable strategem after all.

"Colborn, Unn...those spells, Greater Scrying and Greater Teleport, what's the range?" I asked carefully.

Unn shrugged. "There isn't one. They'll reach as far as you like."

I nodded thoughtfully, feeling a little sick. God, it really would work. I knew how to destroy the Lich Kings and their phylacteries, but the amount of collateral damage would make everyone I'd killed so far into less than a rounding error. Good lord; how twisted did I have to be to conceive of such a thing and, even worse, what did it say about me that even as I was worrying about that, the back of my brain was already working out the practical details?

I looked around for an alternative or a way to delay and, thankfully, found one.

"Well, I've got a couple ideas but they haven't really gelled yet," I lied. "When we have Herrún resurrected, she should be able to help us find the phylacteries. In the meantime, let's focus on more immediate issues. What about converting more of their vampire casters, the way we converted Colborn?"

Albrecht studied me for a moment, deciding whether or not to allow the topic change. Finally he shrugged. "Possible, but difficult. When we were scrying for Colborn, I knew him well enough to draw an accurate picture of him, and I had several of his hairs, so—"

Colborn stared. "You had my hair? How did you get my hair?"

Albrecht grinned. "Remember that oil painting you made for my birthday when you were nine?"

Colborn frowned, then got it. "Oh lord, that horrible thing? You kept that? Really?"

Albrecht laughed. "Yep. Turns out, it was at the bottom of one of my storage chests, along with some other family mementos. Fortunately, you were a lousy artisan; not only was it lopsided and muddy, not only did it have lots of fingerprints where you tried to rub out mistakes, but it had no less than six strands of hair stuck in the oil."

Colborn laughed. "Saved by my younger self's lack of talent! Ah, the irony."

Everyone smiled politely. "In any case," Albrecht continued. "We had a good picture and a physical part of his body, which made it easy to scry him. He also happened to be off on his own when we scried him, so we were able to teleport your Landguard right on top of him for the capture. We aren't likely to get that advantage again."

"Hmm," I mused. "Colborn, Hammond and his boys don't know that you've been turned yet, right?"

He shook his head. "They shouldn't. I was off on a hunt-and-capture against some local refugee bands; I wasn't expected to report in for another two days."

"How many junior casters did Hammond have?" I asked.

He shrugged. "I don't know exactly, but Hammond was one of the weakest Lich Kings. Probably not more than five or ten thousand casters of High Mage level or above, and I'm only aware of a couple hundred Master High Magi." He wiggled his fingers dismissively with a slight sneer. "And, of course, plenty of guppies, but they don't matter much."

Thomas raised an eyebrow. "Guppies?"

Unn sighed. "Low- and midlevel magi. It's not a terribly polite term," she noted with a reproving glance at Colborn, which utterly failed to make a dent in his ego.

I nodded, thinking about the implications. Arrogant jerk he might be, but Colborn was an enormously powerful wizard. If Hammond had two hundred more of his power level, and Hammond was one of the weakest of our enemies...life was not so happy-making for the home team. "If you found yourself engaged in a major battle and needed to call for backup, how would that work?"

Colborn's brow wrinkled up as he frowned. For a moment he reminded me of a Buffy vampire with the nose ridges, but I quickly pushed that thought aside.

"In a hundred and seventeen years, I've never needed to call for backup," Colborn said icily.

"It's a hypothetical, Colborn," Albrecht assured him. "I suspect that Jake is thinking about how to make some of Hammond's casters come to us, instead of us having to go to them."

Colborn sniffed, but went with it. "Well," he began reluctantly. "Hammond's protocol is to use a Sending to notify his Watch Captain of the need for backup and the reason. The Watch Captain then chooses who to send in response and dispatches Sendings to the appropriate casters. We were all required to have a Greater Teleport memorized at all times; if we got a dispatch order, we were to teleport to the necessary location within three rounds of receiving the order—that allows time for casting buffs first."

"What if the order sent you to a location you weren't familiar with? How would you teleport there?" asked Thomas curiously.

Colborn shrugged. "Typically you would teleport to the caster that needed backup. For example, 'Greater Teleport: one hundred feet to the west of so-and-so'."

I nodded, thinking. "How many of those Helms of Opposite Alignment do we have? And can we make more?"

Colborn, being newly arrived in Grǫfhamr, looked to Unn. She thought for a moment and then shrugged. "I think there's two more; they've never been common. And yes, we can make more; it needs a twelfth level mage and four days."

Thomas knew where I was going and asked the question before I could. "How many magi twelfth level or higher do we have?"

Unn thought about that for a second. "If you need an exact count, I'd need to check my records, but it's about fifteen thousand."

I smiled grimly. "Good. So, let's make fifteen thousand Helms. In four days, we can see about taking fifteen thousand high-level casters away from the Lich Kings. That should make it a little harder for them to find us."

Colborn snorted.

"What?" I asked in confuzzlement.

"Once they know we're out here, the Lich Kings won't have any trouble finding us," he declared.

Drifa frowned. "Why not? We've been careful."

"This place is protected by a Weirdstone," Colborn pointed out. "It blocks teleportation within a six mile radius. I'm not sure whose army it was that was camped on your doorstep when you were in Snorri's cavern, but whoever it was, they must have discovered that teleportation was blocked in the area. They could have rapidly located the city itself by working around the perimeter, checking where teleportation worked and where it didn't. As soon as they had a reasonable arc defined, it would be trivial to figure out where the center was."

Everyone froze as we thought through the implications. The five separate 'thunks' of our stomachs hitting the floor formed a lovely, syncopated beat that sounded vaguely like a samba.

"Ok, so once they know the general area they can localize us," I began, looking for a silver lining. "But as long as they can't find our general location...."

Albrecht shook his head, looking pale. "No. It's worse than that. The Weirdstone wards a twelve mile diameter area. Given a quarter million High Magi, you could divide the entire continent into a grid ten miles on a side and try a Greater Teleport into each gridsquare. As soon as one of the attempts fails, you've found a warded area. And Afi alone has well over a million High Magi."

We all took a moment to truly appreciate the depth of the hosedness that was our life. The degree to which we were screwed. The fullness of our doom. The nuances of the 'you-will-die'nesses that were currently piling on top of us. The full flavor of—

Thomas cleared his throat; hope and courage flooded through us all as he pushed his own courage and faith out to the rest of us. Even Colborn sat up straight, eyes brightening.

"Well screw Afi!" I declared firmly. "We'll just have to jam his radar. And I know how...."

As the leadership of the bizarre city that was Grǫfhamr planned and plotted, events were unfolding elsewhere in the city.

"Move, move, move, move!" bellowed the leather-lunged Sergeant Thorn. His voice echoed through the corridors of Grǫfhamr, blurring off into the distance.

People were tumbling out of their Rope Tricks, leaving the corridors jam-packed and all but impassable. The commands echoed down the halls, rolling outwards from the Sergeant's position on a wave of relayed orders. One person would shout the order; a hundred segments away the next person would shout it down the next section of corridor. As the orders rippled by, more and more people were dropping out of their extradimensional cubbies and standing ready for the next stage.

As always, talking was a free action so, as large as Grǫfhamr was, it took only a single round to pass the orders to the farthest corners of the vast city. Once every corridor was full, new orders were passed, and the strangest bucket brigade in the history of the world swung into operation.

It is, perhaps, worth considering the nature of the City Of Holes. Take a long, straight tunnel, one hundred miles long. Break it into segments of various lengths. Stick these segments to each other at right angles, with no particular plan as to how they are arranged. Since each segment is in its own extradimensional space, no branch crosses with any other branch, so reaching a particular area requires walking back up to the nearest common tunnel, then walking all the way back down. This is Grǫfhamr.

There was one saving grace: after the city was assembled, each Hole had a colored number painted on its wall for identification purposes. This made it possible to refer unambiguously to any location in the city, although directions became a bit convoluted. ("Go to White #3781, then take the lefthand branch to Yellow #4017, then the righthand branch to Red #91 and you're there.") Still, identifiable or not, there was little rhyme or reason to the way the city was assembled.

Rhyme and reason were being applied with a vengeance.

About seventy linear miles from Grǫfhamr's exit, a young woman named Hilda (who secretly pined for one of the boys in the adjoining sleep group, and was frustrated that he wouldn't take the damn hint and ask her out), peeled Portable Hole Green #6329 off the air in front of her and dropped it into the waiting hands of her father, Ulfric, who stood behind her. Ulfric (who was fully aware of Hilda's crush, and was torn between giving the boy the hint or threatening him with grievous bodily harm should he so much as look at Hilda) dropped it into the waiting hands of the woman behind him...who dropped it into the waiting hands of the person behind her....

Six seconds later, the Portable Hole in question arrived at White #1 (more commonly known as 'the exit to Grǫfhamr'), and was carefully spread on the lefthand wall, thereby relocating ten linear miles of tunnel and about a million people. An instant later Violet #8972, the segment of Grǫfhamr that began thirty miles from the door, arrived and was spread against the righthand wall.

And so it continued. Each segment of Grǫfhamr was split into thirds, the first third placed on the lefthand wall of its parent, the second third on the righthand wall, and the final third allowed to continue forward. The process was then repeated, again and again and again, iterating on each of the sub-branches. Steadily, the distance from Grǫfhamr's entrance to its furthest point shrank as segments were shifted and reconfigured to fit into the minimum distance.

It wasn't a smooth process. People were lazy, or recalcitrant, or clumsy, or foolish. Sometimes people refused to come down from their sleep area and join the human chain. Sometimes the Portable Holes were dropped on the floor, interrupting the steady flow. (Fortunately, the corridors were too crowded for the Holes to land flat and reopen as a pit; the resulting miles-long fall would have killed hundreds of thousands.) Sometimes people argued instead of simply handing the Holes along. Many people wanted an explanation. And sometimes the orders got distorted in the process of being relayed so many times. Paiman Kaspersen, formerly a highly paid memory-sculptor, found himself very puzzled as to why he was being told to "Bull the stole off the ball at Purple-Yellow #6736 and gas it slack!"

In many cases, these issues could be sorted out by the people on the spot. In a few cases, where fights had actually broken out, stronger measures were needed. When a segment failed to appear within three rounds, Sergeant Thorn would dispatch a Landguard to investigate. That Landguard would be lifted (generally with some grunting) by the nearest Grǫfhamrite and dropped into the hands of the next...and the next...and the next...effectively crowdsurfing along the commoner railgun that had been formed through the length and breadth of the city. In this fashion, a Landguard could travel dozens of miles in a single round. High Marshal Löfgren might believe that it was impossible to teleport inside Grǫfhamr, but he had never encountered the true power of Internet forums filled with D&D players and their warped imaginations.

Once a Landguard appeared at the scene of a problem, that problem hurriedly ceased to exist. Whether the issue was misunderstood orders, people refusing to work, or anything else, Flobovia's elite soldiers resolved it quickly and efficiently. At which point they would explain the importance of what was happening, thank the people in the area for helping, and crowd-surf back to the entrance.

While the majority of the populace were involved in reconfiguring their modular city, a select few were outside working on another project.

The first step had been to have one of the magi cast Mage's Private Sanctum on the ravine where Grǫfhamr was currently located. Thus freed from the fear of being scried upon while they worked, a flood of craftsmen poured forth and began building a half dozen altars to the Lady of Secrets. Sturdy tables of wood or stone were formed, each intricately engraved with runes and writings taken from Master High Priestess Drifa's parchments. None of the workmen understood what those runes and writings meant, so the carving process went painfully slowly as each crafter carefully double- and triple-checked to be certain that each rune or letter was exactly as it should be. The process was slowed even further by the fears of the Grǫfhamrites. After living cheek-by-jowl for so long, most of them had begun to acquire some agoraphobia; working in the relatively open ravine was a living nightmare for them.

Fortunately, those members of the Landguard's Third Company not being used as a mobile reserve by Sergeant Thorn were on hand for the construction. Some fanned out to stand sentry duty while others stood ready to help; their massive strength made shifting giant stones into position the work of moments, and they were always willing to hold something, measure something, or whatever else was needed. Most importantly, as soon as they noticed the difficulties of the artisans, they called on their status as paladins of the True God to lend their own courage and faith to those around them. Freed of their fears, the artisans thanked the Landguard gratefully and buckled down to work.

Guardian Fourth Justin Wineholt was a 'tadpole,' a newly-minted Landguard in his first year after intake. Seventeen years old, he had grown up as a marsh hunter on the edge of the Fens in Flobovia. Today he found himself on a separate continent, one of the last survivors of the 'Guard after fighting his way through a literal ocean of undead, and living inside a crazy city made of extradimensional spaces that was packed with more people than he knew existed. Truth to tell, it was freaking him out; as a child of the marshes, he was used to lots of open space around him, few people, and relative quiet. A wild time at home used to be when all twenty eight people who lived within a day's walk of his parents would get together for music and dancing. Capital City had been a painful adjustment; Grǫfhamr had him praying to the True God for strength at least once an hour.

Given all that, being outside in a narrow ravine with a small strip of cloudy sky overhead was heaven, even if he did have to lug eight-hundred pound boulders from place to place while overstressed artisans yelled at him to hurry it up.

Under the circumstances, Justin could perhaps be forgiven for being discommoded when one of his Landguard cohorts—a Guardian First named Jasmine, who happened to be his swordmistress—raced out of the city and proceeded to slap a Portable Hole on the ravine's opposite wall. The Hole opened to reveal more of Grǫfhamr, the tunnels packed full of people seething about like maddened ants. Jasmine was promptly handed a Portable Hole by the person at the door; she raced back across the ravine and applied this to the opposite wall, next to the first.

As this new Hole opened, the people inside stumbled as though the world had tipped around them. "Oy, watch it!" shouted someone near the entrance. "Open it straight, you dizzy bint! You just shifted the gravity five degrees—any more and you could've killed someone!"

Justin shuddered. Great. Now the damn city was multiplying, and even gravity was something he couldn't count on. Miserable, he went back to hauling stones.
